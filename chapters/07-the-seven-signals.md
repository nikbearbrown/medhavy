## Learning leaves traces

Chapter 6 closed with a category: behavioral traces of cognitive
engagement. This chapter is about what those traces actually look like
and how they distinguish a student who learned something from a student
who produced something that looked like learning.

The neuroscience of the claim is older than Medhavy and uncontroversial.
In 1997, Wolfram Schultz, Peter Dayan, and Read Montague published a
paper in *Science* describing what dopamine neurons in primate brains
actually signal. The result was surprising. Dopamine neurons do not
signal reward itself. They signal the *prediction error* between
expected reward and actual reward (Schultz, Dayan, and Montague,
*Science*, 275, 1997). When something happens that you expected, the
dopamine signal is muted. When something violates your expectation —
surprise, pleasant or unpleasant — the signal is strong. The
prediction-error signal is what tells the brain *this is worth
encoding*.

Translate this into a learning context. A student who works through a
problem, makes a wrong prediction, and is corrected has generated a
prediction error. The error is the encoding signal. A student who asks
an AI for the answer, copies the answer, and submits it has not made a
prediction. There is no error. There is no encoding signal. The student
has produced an artifact and bypassed the neurobiological event that
constitutes learning.

This is the mechanism beneath the Bastani finding. The unguarded GPT-4
group produced fluent practice performance because the AI did the
problem-solving — bypassing prediction error. The control group made
predictions, was sometimes wrong, was corrected, and encoded. Six weeks
later, the encoded knowledge held; the bypassed knowledge was never
encoded in the first place.

What follows from this mechanism is that genuine learning leaves
*behavioral* traces. Not always large traces. Not always traces you
can see in the artifact. But traces in the behavior around the
artifact's production: how time was spent, whether errors followed
coherent patterns, whether the knowledge held at delay, whether a
small hint unlocked an emerging understanding. Robert and Elizabeth
Bjork's distinction between storage strength and retrieval strength
(Bjork and Bjork, 1992, in *From Learning Processes to Cognitive
Processes*) is the foundational framework for these traces; thirty
years of empirical work has filled in the details across the specific
signals you will read about in a moment.

The Frictional Framework is Medhavy's name for the integrated
measurement architecture built around these behavioral traces. The
seven signals — Y1 through Y7 — are the specific traces Medhavy
measures. The GLP score is the composite. All three terms are
proprietary to Humanitarians AI. [Humanitarians AI internal
framework. No outside-of-Medhavy primary source uses *Frictional
Framework*, *seven signals*, or *GLP score* in this exact form. The
underlying mechanistic literature, cited below for each signal, is not
proprietary.]

The chapter's intellectually honest framing: each individual signal
draws on a well-established body of research. The integration — the
specific seven, in this combination, with this scoring rubric — is
Medhavy's bet. The reader is buying that integration on theoretical
grounds when they adopt. The components have evidence. The composite
is, as of 2026, theoretically motivated rather than empirically
validated at scale.

## The seven signals

What follows is each signal in plain language: name, mechanism in one
sentence, an example of what the signal looks like in a health
sciences setting, and the mode that primarily produces it.

### Y1 — Temporal Engagement

*Mechanism: harder material requires more processing time when the
processing is genuine; offloaded processing decouples time from
difficulty.*

The cognitive load theory tradition (John Sweller, *Cognitive
Science*, 12(2), 1988) gives the mechanism. Real cognitive work takes
time, and the time scales with the intrinsic difficulty of the
material. A student who genuinely processes a harder pharmacology
case spends longer on it than on an easier case in the same module.
A student who has offloaded the work — to an AI, to a copied answer,
to a vague guess — does not show that scaling. The harder problem
takes about the same time as the easier one, because the work is not
happening.

Y1 is not just total time; it is the *distribution* of time across
problems of varying difficulty. A flat distribution is a warning sign.
A scaling distribution is consistent with genuine processing. Ask AI
and Case Study primarily produce Y1, because both involve enough
moment-to-moment engagement that time distribution is informative.

### Y2 — Error Trajectory Coherence

*Mechanism: real misconceptions produce coherent errors that update
lawfully under correct instruction; guessing produces random,
non-updating errors.*

This is John Seely Brown and Richard Burton's 1978 finding, originally
in elementary arithmetic. They showed that student errors are not
random. They reveal systematic procedural misconceptions — *bugs* in
the student's mental model — that update lawfully when the student
encounters correct instruction (Brown and Burton, *Cognitive Science*,
2(2), 1978). Kurt VanLehn's *Mind Bugs* (1990) is the book-length
treatment. The pattern is robust across domains.

In nursing: a student who has not yet learned to integrate vital-signs
trends will consistently misread subtle deterioration as stable. The
errors will be *coherent* — same pattern in similar cases — and will
*update* when corrected: after instruction, the student stops making
that specific kind of error. A student guessing on the same scenarios
makes incoherent errors that do not update. The trajectory of the
errors over time is the signal. Quiz Me and Case Study primarily
produce Y2, because both generate enough scored attempts to make the
trajectory visible.

### Y3 — Cross-Context Transfer

*Mechanism: genuine understanding applies to new contexts because the
underlying mechanism was encoded; surface learning does not transfer
because only the surface features were encoded.*

This is the oldest learning-science question — first asked by Edward
Thorndike in 1901, who came down hard on the side of *transfer is rare*
(Thorndike, *Psychological Review*, 8, 1901). The modern reframing is
from John Bransford and Daniel Schwartz in 1999: think of transfer not
as direct application but as *preparation for future learning* — the
student who genuinely understood the original mechanism is faster to
learn the next, related mechanism (Bransford and Schwartz, *Review of
Research in Education*, 24, 1999). Barnett and Ceci, in 2002, gave the
taxonomy of near and far transfer (Barnett and Ceci, *Psychological
Bulletin*, 128(4), 2002).

In pharmacy: a student who genuinely learned the mechanism of QT
prolongation on one drug class can recognize the risk in a new drug
class. The student who memorized the warning labels cannot. Cross-
context recognition is the signal. Case Study and Glimmer primarily
produce Y3, because both deploy learning in contexts that differ from
the acquisition context.

### Y4 — Uncertainty Calibration

*Mechanism: genuine learners have well-calibrated metacognition —
their confidence tracks their actual knowledge; AI-assisted students
inherit the AI's confidence without the underlying knowledge.*

The metacognition literature is John Dunlosky and Janet Metcalfe's
domain. Their 2008 textbook *Metacognition* is the accessible summary;
the foundational paper is Nelson and Narens (1990), *Psychology of
Learning and Motivation*, 26. The finding: students systematically
mis-estimate their own learning, and the direction of the
mis-estimation depends on the study technique. Borrowed certainty —
using an external resource during study — inflates judgments of
learning without improving recall. The student feels more confident
than the test reveals to be warranted.

The signal in practice: a genuine learner who has studied
anticoagulation correctly will hesitate on the borderline case — the
patient with mild hepatic dysfunction and low body weight — and will
say *I am not sure; I would want to check the most recent guideline*.
An AI-assisted student who absorbed a textbook answer will state the
answer with a confidence the situation does not warrant. The
hesitation is the signal of calibration. Ask AI, Case Study, and
Glimmer primarily produce Y4, because each requires the student to
make and express confidence judgments.

### Y5 — Social Knowledge Texture

*Mechanism: genuine engagement produces specific confusions, partial
agreements, and real-time position changes in dialogue; borrowed
certainty produces smooth, generic statements.*

This signal is less standardized in the literature than the others
and leans on Allyson Hadwin and Sanna Järvelä's work on socially
shared regulation of learning (Hadwin and Järvelä, *Teachers College
Record*, 2011), as well as the broader case-discussion tradition.
The mechanism: a student reasoning through a case in dialogue — with
an AI, a peer, or an instructor — leaves a textured trace. Specific
confusions are named. Positions change in response to argument.
Half-formed ideas are articulated and revised. A student who has not
engaged but is producing dialogue from absorbed text produces smooth,
generic statements with no specific confusion and no position change.

This is the signal you have observed informally in case discussions
for years. Case Study and Glimmer primarily produce Y5, because both
involve dialogue or written argumentation where texture is observable.

### Y6 — Retrieval Strength Decay

*Mechanism: genuine learning produces durable storage; retrieval
strength may decay with disuse but storage strength does not; borrowed
performance has no storage to decay from.*

This is the Bjork distinction again, stated mechanistically. Robert
and Elizabeth Bjork in 1992 distinguished *storage strength* (how
well the trace is laid down) from *retrieval strength* (how easily
the trace can be accessed at a given moment). Retrieval strength
decays with disuse; storage strength does not. The cleanest
experimental demonstration is Karpicke and Roediger (*Science*, 319,
2008): students who tested themselves once outperformed students who
restudied four times at a one-week delay. Henry Bahrick's 1984 work
on *permastore* showed the same pattern at the fifty-year scale
(Bahrick, *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*, 113(1),
1984): what survives twenty-five years after Spanish class is not
what scored well immediately.

The signal in practice: a student who genuinely learned the
differential for chest pain in the cardiology block can still produce
it three months later in the emergency medicine block, even if
slowly. A student who memorized for the cardiology exam cannot. The
delayed retrieval check is the signal. Quiz Me primarily produces Y6,
because the FSRS scheduling structurally tests retrieval at
increasing intervals.

### Y7 — Scaffolding Response Curve

*Mechanism: a learner with genuine partial understanding responds to
a partial hint by completing the reasoning; a learner with no
underlying understanding needs the full answer.*

This is Vygotsky's zone of proximal development, made measurable.
Vygotsky in 1978 (*Mind in Society*) described the zone where a
learner can do, with help, what they cannot yet do alone. Anders
Ericsson and colleagues in 1993 (*Psychological Review*, 100(3))
formalized deliberate practice as effortful work at the edge of
current capability. Koedinger and Aleven in 2007 (*Educational
Psychology Review*, 19) named the *assistance dilemma* — too much
help produces offloading, too little produces shutdown — and
established that the response shape to graduated hints is itself
diagnostic.

The signal: a student who has partial understanding will, given a
small hint, complete the reasoning and arrive at the answer. A
student without underlying understanding will not move forward
from the same hint. The shape of the student's response to
graduated assistance reveals where the student is in the zone. Ask
AI and Case Study primarily produce Y7, because both use graduated
hint structures.

*Reading:* **Primary** means the mode is designed to produce this
signal as its main measurement output. **Secondary** means the signal
is observable as a side effect of the mode but is not the mode's
primary measurement target. A dash means the mode does not
meaningfully produce that signal.

A few things to notice. Case Study is *primary* on six of the seven
signals. This is not a marketing accident — it is the structural
reason Case Study is the highest-information mode. A student running
through a Case Study with the AI facilitator is generating signal
across calibration, error trajectory, transfer, scaffolding response,
social texture, and temporal engagement simultaneously. The mode is
expensive to produce (faculty review, scenario authoring, graduated
hint design) because it is doing the most work. Quiz Me, in contrast,
is the cleanest single-signal mode: Y6 retrieval strength decay is
what it is built to measure. The trade-off is clarity for breadth.

The mapping itself is a Medhavy design choice. Each cell has face
validity given each mode's structure, but the mapping has not been
empirically validated as a deployed measurement architecture. The
chapter is honest about that. The mapping is the framework the loop
uses; whether the framework is the optimal seven-signal-by-four-mode
assignment is an open question that future deployment data will
answer. [Humanitarians AI internal framework.]

## What the GLP score is not

The GLP score is a composite of the seven signals: per student, per
concept, updated as more interactions accumulate. The score appears
on a dashboard the instructor sees. It is, in TIKTOC's framing, an
*evidence layer* — a number with constituent signals visible beneath
it.

The score is not a grade. It is not a substitute for faculty
judgment. The TIKTOC anatomy is explicit on this point: the instructor
is the meta-model. The instructor weighs the GLP score against the
artifact quality, against direct observation in class and clinic,
against everything else the instructor knows about the student. The
score adds an input the instructor currently cannot see; it does not
replace the inputs the instructor already has.

This framing matters because the alternative — a GLP score as
gradebook entry — would import all the pathologies of the gradebook
into the new measurement layer. Students would optimize against the
GLP score. The score would be gamed. Its validity, which depends on
the score being a measurement of something behavioral and not a target
the student is consciously trying to hit, would collapse. Medhavy's
design choice is to keep the GLP score in the evidence layer where
faculty see it but students do not directly optimize against it.
Whether this is operationally sustainable as students become aware of
the seven-signal structure is an open question. The design choice is
the right one for the architecture. The operational sustainability is
unknown. [Humanitarians AI internal framework.]

The GLP score is also not a fixed number. It updates as more
interactions accumulate. Early in the term, the score is noisy. By
mid-term, the constituent signals have stabilized enough that the
composite is interpretable. The cold-start period — when the score
is too noisy to act on — is real and is one of the honest limits of
the system. Chapter 8 covers this in the bandit context.

## Worked example — two students through seven signals

Return to the two students from Chapter 6. Same Canvas analytics:
forty-seven minutes on the module, one hundred and forty-ish clicks,
one hundred percent completion, eighty-eight percent on the quiz,
submitted assignment of similar surface quality. Canvas could not
distinguish them. Here is what the seven signals see.

| Signal                       | Student A (genuine)         | Student B (offloaded)         |
|------------------------------|-----------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Y1 Temporal Engagement       | Time scales with difficulty | Flat across difficulty        |
| Y2 Error Trajectory          | Coherent, updating          | Random, non-updating          |
| Y3 Cross-Context Transfer    | Applies in new scenario     | Does not transfer             |
| Y4 Uncertainty Calibration   | Hesitates on edge cases     | Confident on edge cases       |
| Y5 Social Knowledge Texture  | Specific confusions         | Smooth, generic statements    |
| Y6 Retrieval Strength Decay  | Holds at 6 weeks            | Collapses at 6 weeks          |
| Y7 Scaffolding Response      | Unlocks with partial hint   | Needs full answer             |

Student A's signal profile reads as someone who genuinely engaged with
the material. The time distribution scales. Errors form a coherent
pattern that updates with feedback. Knowledge transfers to new
contexts. Confidence tracks knowledge. Dialogue has specific texture.
Retrieval holds at delay. Partial hints unlock. Each signal is one
input; the composite is the GLP score; the score is high.

Student B's signal profile reads as offloading. Time is decoupled
from difficulty. Errors do not update. No transfer. Borrowed
certainty. Smooth dialogue. Retrieval collapses. Hints do not unlock.
Each signal is one input; the composite is low.

The Canvas dashboard, six weeks earlier, said both students were
indistinguishable. The seven-signal profile, taken in the moment,
predicted the difference six weeks before the difference became
visible on the unaided retention quiz. This is what *behavioral
traces of cognitive engagement* means in practice.

Worth flagging again: the example is illustrative, not from a
published validation study of the seven-signal composite as a
predictive measurement. The components are evidence-based. The
composite's predictive validity, at the scale Medhavy proposes to
operate, is the bet you are evaluating. The chapter's earlier
honesty about this is the chapter's honest position.

## Common misconceptions

**"The seven signals are just better engagement metrics."** They are
not. Engagement metrics measure interaction with the platform.
The seven signals measure traces of cognitive process: how time
distributes across difficulty, whether errors update, whether
knowledge transfers, whether confidence calibrates. The difference is
the difference Chapter 6 names — engagement evidence versus learning
evidence. Better click counts will not produce Y3.

**"The GLP score is the new gradebook."** It is not. The GLP score is
an evidence layer the instructor reads; the gradebook is what gets
recorded. Faculty grade. The score informs the grade. Treating the
score as the grade would gameify the signals and collapse their
validity.

**"If we measure seven things instead of five, we are doing seven-
fifths better."** No. The signals are not commensurable with engagement
metrics. They are answering a categorically different question. The
shift from five engagement metrics to seven learning signals is not a
quantitative upgrade. It is a different measurement category.

**"The seven signals are validated."** Each individual signal has
substantial empirical support from cognitive psychology — Y6 retrieval
decay is forty years old; Y2 error coherence is forty-eight years old;
Y3 transfer is one hundred and twenty-five years old. The *composite*
— the specific seven, integrated as a deployed measurement system —
is Medhavy's bet. The reader should know they are adopting a
theoretically motivated framework, not an empirically validated
deployed score.

**"Why seven?"** Because Medhavy committed to seven. Could plausibly be
six (Y1 and Y4 cluster as in-the-moment calibration signals; they
could be one) or nine (social texture could split into discussion-
specific and writing-specific signals). The number is an architectural
choice. The chapter does not defend the number. It explains why each
of the seven is in the set.

## What would change my mind

A published, replicated demonstration that a five-engagement-metric
composite predicts delayed retention as well as the seven-signal
composite would undermine the chapter's central claim. The case for
the seven signals rests on the categorical difference between
engagement evidence and learning evidence. If a sufficiently rich
engagement model recovers learning prediction, the categorical
distinction softens into a resolution distinction, and the
architectural argument for a separate measurement layer weakens. The
component evidence (Bjork, Karpicke, Brown and Burton, Bransford and
Schwartz) would remain in place. The case for the integrated
Frictional Framework as a *distinct* category of measurement would
need to be rebuilt on different grounds.

## Still puzzling

- The mapping table treats *primary* and *secondary* as categorical.
  In a deployed system, these would be continuous weights. The
  weighting rules are proprietary and unspecified in this book. How
  much does the specific weighting affect the GLP score?

- Faculty interpretability of the GLP dashboard has not been studied
  in published work. A score with seven constituent sparklines is
  more information than a single number. Whether typical health
  sciences faculty correctly read the dashboard is an open empirical
  question.

- The signals are described mostly from the Anglophone and European
  cognitive psychology tradition. Whether the same signals look the
  same in culturally diverse cohorts — particularly Y5 social
  knowledge texture, which is heavily dialect- and culture-mediated
  — is genuinely open.

- The cold-start period for the GLP score is real. How long it is in
  practice, for a given concept, depends on student volume and
  signal strength. The book promises *first six sessions* as the
  exploratory window in Chapter 8. Whether this is the right length
  empirically is unknown.

## Bridge

The seven signals are what Medhavy measures. Measurement on its own
does not change anything. What changes things is what the system does
with the measurement: presents differently, sees the signal, adapts
the presentation, repeats. That is the loop. The contextual bandit is
the algorithm that runs the loop. Chapter 8 explains it in plain
language, walks through what the bandit does over eight weeks for one
concept and one student, and names the honest limits.
