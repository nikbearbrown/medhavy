# Chapter 11 — The Adoption Decision

**One-line capability:** The reader produces their justified adoption recommendation — for or against, with specific reasoning — and knows what success looks like if the answer is yes.

---

## Opening

This chapter does not make the decision for you. It gives you the structure to make it yourself and the language to explain it to a budget committee.

The temptation in a final chapter is to summarize the book. This chapter does something different. The reader who has reached chapter eleven does not need the modes re-explained or the loop re-walked. She needs the artifact she will use — the one-page recommendation that goes to her budget committee — and she needs to know what success looks like if she signs the contract, what failure looks like if she signs the wrong contract, and what the honest case for not signing looks like written as a confident professional recommendation rather than a hedge.

The chapter is organized as the path through that artifact. The decision tool comes first. The four conditions that surround it come after — what success looks like, what failure looks like, what the honest case for no looks like, and how to talk to the budget committee. The closing returns the reader to the book's thesis.

A note on how experienced decision-makers actually decide. The decision scientist Gerd Gigerenzer spent his career studying expert judgment under uncertainty — emergency room physicians, military commanders, investors making calls without complete information. His finding, repeated across many domains, was that experts do not weigh many options against many criteria. They recognize the situation as a familiar pattern and check whether one well-chosen course of action holds up under inspection. The three-question test from Chapter 9 is exactly that kind of recognition aid. Once the pattern is named, the decision tends to fall out. The chapter's job is not to engineer a perfect optimization. It is to give the reader a structure to check her own recognition against, so she can defend it to a committee that has not done the recognition work.

## Core content

### Block 1 — The decision framework assembled

The decision framework is everything the book has built, brought into one artifact the reader can fill in and put in front of a committee.

The reader has the three questions from Chapter 9. She has the mode-to-need matching from Chapters 3 through 5 — Ask AI for acquisition, Quiz Me for durable retention, Case Study for application under ambiguity, Glimmer for generative work. She has the institutional requirements from Chapter 10 — concept map, faculty review of cases, willingness to act with a named owner and budgeted time. She has the timeline from Chapter 10 — ten to twelve weeks from decision to first data, paced by institutional review rather than by IT.

The artifact assembles those four into a single document the reader writes herself. The format is hybrid — structured prose with fixed fields, plus an optional side-by-side comparison table for readers who are weighing more than one deployment. The format is deliberate. A pure table implies more deterministic decision-making than the situation warrants. A pure flowchart hides the nuance. A pure prose memo is hard to scan in a committee meeting. The hybrid lets the reader think in prose and the committee scan in fields.

**The adoption decision tool — six fields**

> **Field 1 — Deployment under evaluation (one sentence).**
> Name the deployment, the enrollment, the section structure, the instructor staffing, and the strategic pressure (accreditation, board pass, retention, etc.) in one tight sentence.
>
> *Example: "Pharmacology service course, 320 students across four sections, three instructors with varying experience, shared final exam, accreditation review next year flagging inter-section variance in board pass rates."*
>
> **Field 2 — Three-question answers and reasoning (three short paragraphs, one per question).**
> For each question, state the answer (yes / no / contingent) and the one or two sentences of reasoning that drives it. The reasoning is more important than the answer; a yes you cannot defend is not a yes. Name the specific feature of the deployment driving each answer.
>
> *Example for Q3: "Yes, contingent. The chair has authority and has stated publicly that the underperforming section will be redesigned. The precommitment is not yet in writing. Field will become unambiguous yes upon written agreement among the three instructors, expected before contract signing."*
>
> **Field 3 — What Medhavy would add that Canvas does not (two to three sentences, jargon-free).**
> The committee version of the loop, in plain language. No acronyms. No mode names. What does the institution gain in operational terms? What question does it answer that the current stack does not?
>
> *Example: "Canvas tells us which students submitted, when, and at what score. It does not tell us which students learned the material well enough to retrieve it six weeks later without the textbook in front of them. Medhavy measures that, per student per concept, and adapts how the material is presented based on what it measures."*
>
> **Field 4 — First-term success criteria (measurable, time-bounded).**
> Two to three specific outcomes that, if achieved by end of the first or second term, would justify renewal. The criteria must be falsifiable. "Improved learning" is not a criterion. "Section-to-section board pass-rate gap narrows by 50% by end of term 2" is.
>
> *Example: "Section-to-section pass-rate gap on the shared final narrows by at least 50% by end of term 2. GLP retrieval strength signals at week 14 predict final exam performance at r > 0.4 in the pilot section. Faculty self-report on the dashboard's actionability scores above 3 on a 5-point scale."*
>
> **Field 5 — Non-renewal triggers (two to three specific outcomes that would end the deployment).**
> The willingness to walk away. SaaS edtech tools mostly fail at renewal, not at adoption — the renewal decision is the honest one. State the conditions under which the institution does not extend the contract.
>
> *Example: "No measurable narrowing of inter-section pass-rate gap by end of term 2. Faculty case-review burden exceeds 30% of original-authorship-equivalent time. Underperforming section is identified but the institution does not act on the data within one term of identification — willingness-to-act condition has lapsed."*
>
> **Field 6 — Honest acknowledgment of uncertainty (one short paragraph).**
> What does the reader not yet know, and how will she find out? This field is not optional. A recommendation that pretends to certainty is less defensible than one that names its uncertainties. The committee should see the reader's honesty here.
>
> *Example: "It is not yet clear whether the three-instructor coordination required for term-2 redesign will hold under the chair's leadership change scheduled for next year. The contract should include a renewal clause keyed to Field 4 outcomes rather than a multi-year auto-renewal. The pilot scope (one section) is deliberately narrow; full-program adoption is a separate decision next year."*

Three rows for the three questions, two columns for the two stacks. ### Block 2 — What success looks like if the answer is yes

Success in the first term is not "GLP scores improved." It is the specific outcome the deployment was for.

For the pharmacology service course in the worked example: success is the inter-section pass-rate gap narrowing. The shared final exam is the measurement instrument the institution already has, calibrated to a standard the institution already accepts. Medhavy's job is not to invent new measurements. It is to produce evidence that lets the chair narrow the gap that the existing measurements have already documented.

What the GLP signals should show, by week ten of a fifteen-week term: per-concept patterns that match the chair's intuition where she has one (her two-decade experience tells her receptor pharmacology is the rate-limiting concept; the GLP retrieval-strength signals at concept-node level confirm it) and that surprise her where she does not (her intuition said dosing decisions were the hardest; the temporal engagement and uncertainty calibration signals point her to drug-drug interactions instead). The dashboard does not contradict her judgment. It adds a second voice to it.

What the bandit's per-concept policy should show, by end of term: differentiation between students that the gradebook does not show. Maria and Marcus both scored 86% on the unit two exam. The bandit's policy for Maria has converged on Ask AI plus Quiz Me, with high retrieval strength at delay; Maria has learned. The policy for Marcus has converged on Case Study plus Glimmer with weak temporal engagement on Quiz Me; Marcus is performing but not retaining. The chair sees the difference. The Canvas gradebook does not show it.

What would justify renewal at end of year one: Field 4 outcomes met (the measurable, time-bounded criteria the reader committed to before signing). Faculty using the dashboard with greater than fifty percent regularity in the second half of term — meaning the technology is actually integrated into practice, not licensed and abandoned. At least one instance of action taken on the data — a section redesigned, a concept reordered, a Case Study revised — within term two.

What would not justify renewal: Field 4 outcomes missed despite good faith. Faculty dashboard usage trending toward zero. The chair never having to act on the data because the data never showed anything actionable, which is information that either Q1 or Q2 was a no in disguise.

The Carnegie Mellon Open Learning Initiative remains the best public example of an adaptive university platform with longitudinal learning outcome data — their published results show real gains under conditions of intensive curricular labor. The pattern Medhavy aims to repeat is the same: real gains where the curricular labor is staffed; nothing where it is not.

### Block 3 — What failure looks like

There are three documented failure modes for adaptive measurement platforms in higher education. Each one fails Q1, Q2, or Q3 respectively, but in a form that was missed at adoption.

**Failure mode 1 — Wrong deployment context.** The institution adopted Medhavy in a deployment that, run honestly through the three-question test, should have stayed on AI+1 on Kindle. The dashboard sits unused not because anyone is incompetent but because the instructor is already adapting in real time and the system is producing a slower, more expensive copy of what she already knew. This is the nursing-fundamentals case from Chapter 9, adopted anyway because the budget existed and the proposal looked compelling. The honest signal that this failure is happening: by end of term one, the instructor cannot name three specific decisions she made based on the dashboard that she would not have made without it. The fix is non-renewal, not iteration.

**Failure mode 2 — Insufficient curriculum design.** The institution adopted Medhavy and treated the concept map as a fifteen-minute upload of the syllabus. The bandit cannot adapt because the concept map is too coarse. Every concept is just "chapter six." The system flags students as struggling but cannot pinpoint where because the map does not have the resolution. Vendors sometimes encourage this pattern by offering "suggest 15 chapters and we'll generate the map for you." The output is unusable. The fix is to staff the curriculum design layer properly — three sessions of three hours, plus revisions, plus faculty review of generated artifacts — or, if the institution cannot staff it, to accept that the loop will not close and to non-renew.

**Failure mode 3 — Unwillingness to act.** The institution adopted Medhavy, built a real concept map, authored real Case Studies, populated the dashboard. The dashboard works. It flags the underperforming section. The institution does not redesign it. The chair has retired, the senior faculty member whose section is flagged refuses to engage with the data, the new chair does not want to inherit the political fight, and the dashboard goes from being an instrument of redesign to being a museum piece. This is the most common SaaS edtech failure mode, documented across the customer-success literature. The fix is not technological. The fix is institutional, and if the institution cannot find it, the honest move is to non-renew rather than to keep paying for a measurement nobody is using.

The three failure modes correspond exactly to the three questions. Q1 fails: wrong context. Q2 fails: insufficient curriculum design (the measurement was attempted but the institution did not invest enough to make it diagnostic). Q3 fails: unwillingness to act. The pattern is symmetric. The three questions name where adoption succeeds; the three failure modes name where adoption goes wrong.

### Block 4 — The honest case for no

This block is the chapter's hardest paragraph, and the reason this is a final chapter rather than a midpoint chapter. The book has spent ten chapters making the case for what Medhavy is and what it can do. The eleventh chapter has to be willing to recommend not adopting it, in named deployment types, as a confident professional recommendation rather than a hedge.

The case for no is not a compromise. It is the architecturally correct answer in specific contexts:

**Small classes with engaged faculty.** The seminar of twelve. The nursing fundamentals of twenty-eight. The senior elective of forty with a beloved instructor who has taught it for two decades. The instructor's working memory, weekly office hours, and embedded relationship with each student are the adaptation mechanism. The loop is already closed. Adding Medhavy here installs a slower, less responsive parallel system.

**Single-instructor seminars where the instructor designs as she teaches.** The interdisciplinary seminar with a fixed cohort that the instructor revises every offering. The whole point of the seminar is that the instructor is adapting based on this cohort, this term, in ways that do not generalize. The institutional infrastructure that would make Medhavy useful — concept map, case bank, prerequisite graph — would also make the seminar less of what it is.

**Low-heterogeneity cohorts.** Cohort programs where students arrive at the course with similar backgrounds, advance together, and graduate as a class — accelerated nursing, executive MBA cohorts, residency-bound clerkships. The cohort moves as one. Per-student adaptation is solving a problem the cohort structure has already solved.

**Deployments without dedicated curriculum design capacity.** The program that has the budget for the license but not the faculty time for the concept map, the case authoring, and the ongoing curricular maintenance. This is the most common silent failure — the institution that can afford Medhavy in the line-item sense but cannot afford it in the faculty-time sense. The honest recommendation is to invest the available capacity in what the existing stack can do well, not to spread the capacity across a layer it cannot support.

The honest case for no is not "Medhavy is bad." It is "Medhavy is the right answer to a specific question, and your deployment is not asking that question." A curriculum director who can articulate that distinction in a budget meeting is doing the institution a service. The historian Audrey Watters spent a career documenting the educational technology procurement decisions where institutions bought what they could not use. The reader who reaches a no recommendation in this chapter is participating in the discipline Watters' work most respects — refusing to buy what the institution cannot use.

A no recommendation is not less professional than a yes. It is sometimes more professional, because it requires the reader to defend a position the proposal on her desk was written to push back against. Mary Parker Follett, an early-twentieth-century management theorist whose work on integrative decision-making remains underread, called this the "law of the situation": the right course of action is determined by the situation, not by the toolmaker's preference. The reader's situation determines the answer. The book has given her the vocabulary to recognize what her situation is.

### Block 5 — Talking to the budget committee

The budget committee is not optimizing. It is balancing. The reader's job in the two-minute pitch is not to convince them that Medhavy is good. It is to give them the structure to evaluate the recommendation she has made.

The two-minute pitch is three sentences plus one question.

**Sentence one — What it does that Canvas does not.**
"Canvas tells us who submitted, when, and at what score. It does not tell us which students retain the material well enough to use it without the textbook in front of them. Medhavy measures that — per student, per concept — and adapts how each student encounters the material based on what it measures."

**Sentence two — When it is the right answer.**
"It earns its cost in deployments where three things are true: we need to present content differently to different learners at scale, someone needs to know whether the variation is helping, and the institution is prepared to act on what the measurement reveals. Our pharmacology service course meets all three."

**Sentence three — When it is not.**
"For our small classes with engaged faculty, our cohort programs, and the CE modules where completion is what the regulator counts, the existing stack is the right answer and adopting Medhavy would be overhead. The recommendation is targeted to one course, not the program."

**The one question that reveals readiness.**
"If the dashboard shows that one of the four pharmacology sections is producing weaker durable learning than the other three, who at this table is going to call the meeting with the three instructors to redesign that section?"

That question is the test of Q3. If the room can name the person, the deployment is ready. If the room looks at each other, the willingness to act is not yet in place, and the answer to the committee is not "approve" — it is "approve once we have an answer to that question."

The three sentences are top-down — what Minto's writing on executive communication calls bottom line on top. The structure puts the conclusion first, the conditions second, the limits third. The committee gets the recommendation before they get the framework. The framework is in the one-page artifact they can read afterward.

## Worked example — Maria's recommendation

The following is a justified adoption recommendation written in the voice of the book's primary reader — Maria, the curriculum director at a mid-size health sciences program. Her deployment is the pharmacology service course from Chapters 9 and 10. The recommendation is what she will hand the budget committee.

---

**Recommendation: Adopt Medhavy for pilot in PHM 401 Pharmacology, Section 2, beginning fall term.**

**Field 1 — Deployment under evaluation.**
Pharmacology service course (PHM 401), 320 students across four sections, three instructors with varying experience, shared final exam, mid-cycle accreditation review next year flagging inter-section variance in board pass rates as a concern.

**Field 2 — Three-question answers and reasoning.**
Q1 — Yes. Three hundred and twenty students across four sections taught by three instructors who currently do not coordinate weekly. The heterogeneity in prerequisite preparation, scheduling, and instructional approach is real and exceeds any one instructor's working-memory capacity.
Q2 — Yes. The accreditation team needs to know which section and which concepts are driving the variance, which is information the current Canvas analytics cannot produce. The existing final-exam breakdown tells us variance exists. It does not tell us what to redesign.
Q3 — Yes, contingent on written precommitment. As department chair I have committed to redesigning whichever section the data identifies. The two senior faculty members teaching the other two sections have agreed verbally. We are securing written agreement before contract signing. Field will be unambiguous yes upon signature.

**Field 3 — What Medhavy would add that Canvas does not.**
Canvas tells us who completed the module, when, and at what score. It does not tell us which students retain the material six weeks later without the book. Medhavy measures retention and adapts presentation per student per concept, producing the diagnostic evidence the accreditation team has asked for.

**Field 4 — First-term success criteria.**
By end of pilot term (term 2): inter-section pass-rate gap on shared final narrows by at least 50% from current baseline. GLP retrieval-strength signals at week fourteen predict final-exam performance at correlation greater than 0.4. Pilot-section instructor reports dashboard actionability at three or above on a five-point scale, with at least one concrete redesign decision made based on dashboard data within term two.

**Field 5 — Non-renewal triggers.**
We do not renew if any of the following hold at end of term 2: no measurable narrowing of inter-section pass-rate gap; faculty case-review burden exceeds thirty percent of from-scratch authoring time without proportional learning evidence; the underperforming section is identified by the dashboard but no redesign occurs within term three. Contract should be one-year initial, not multi-year auto-renewal.

**Field 6 — Honest acknowledgment of uncertainty.**
I do not yet know whether the three-instructor coordination will survive the chair-leadership transition scheduled for the following academic year. The contract is scoped to one pilot section deliberately; full-program adoption is a separate decision contingent on Field 4 outcomes and on whether the coordination structure holds. I do not know whether the GLP signal evidence will be as diagnostic in our cohort as it has been in the deployments Medhavy has reported on; the first term is partly a test of that question.

---

*Recommendation prepared by curriculum director, in consultation with department chair, IT, and instructional design. Estimated total institutional time: eighty hours faculty (chair plus reviewer), thirty hours instructional designer, six hours IT, twenty hours institutional review. Estimated time from approval to first student in loop: eleven to twelve weeks.*

---

That is the artifact. It is one page when printed. It fits the committee's attention. It is defensible. It is honest. And if the committee asks the chair's one question — who calls the meeting with the three instructors if the data points to one of them — the reader has named that person already (the chair, in writing). The pitch closes itself.

## AI Wayback marginal callout — Mary Parker Follett

There is an early-twentieth-century management theorist worth knowing about when the decision involves a budget committee. **Mary Parker Follett** (1868–1933) wrote about what she called integrative decision-making — finding decisions that genuinely satisfy multiple constituencies rather than compromising between them. Her best-known framing, the "law of the situation," holds that the right course of action is determined by the situation rather than by anyone's preferences, including the toolmaker's. A budget committee evaluating a Medhavy proposal is exactly the kind of multi-constituency situation Follett wrote about: the IT shop has one set of priorities, the faculty have another, the accreditation officer has a third, finance has a fourth, and the integrative answer is the one that respects what the situation actually requires. Follett's work was neglected for most of the twentieth century and has only recently been recovered. The reader walking into the budget meeting with the one-page artifact in hand is doing what Follett described: letting the situation, not the vendor, determine the answer.

## Exercises

**Exercise 1 (Create) — Write your adoption recommendation.**
One page. Use the six fields. Must answer: which deployment are you evaluating, what are your three-question answers and the reasoning behind each, what does Medhavy add that Canvas cannot, what does success look like in measurable terms by end of the first or second term, what would cause non-renewal, and what do you not yet know. If you cannot complete a field, that is information about the decision — name what is missing rather than papering over it. The recommendation is finished when you would be willing to sign your name to it and hand it to a budget committee.

**Exercise 2 (Create) — Write your two-minute pitch.**
Three sentences plus one question. No jargon. No vendor language. Sentence one names what Medhavy does that Canvas does not. Sentence two names when it is the right answer for your institution. Sentence three names when it is not. The question reveals whether the deployment is ready to act on what the measurement reveals. Practice the pitch out loud. If it takes longer than two minutes, it is not yet two-minute pitch ready.

**Exercise 3 (Evaluate) — If your answer is no, write the revisit conditions.**
If your recommendation is against adoption, name the specific conditions under which you would revisit. What would have to change in the deployment context for the three-question answers to flip? An enrollment threshold? A leadership transition? A new accreditation standard? A change in faculty composition? The revisit conditions are the responsible version of "no." They tell the institution when to ask the question again. Without them, "no" reads as "never," which is rarely what the reader actually means.

## Common misconceptions

**"The decision tool will make the decision for me."** It will not. The tool structures the recognition the reader already has. If the reader has not done the three-question work honestly, no decision tool produces a defensible answer. The tool is a memo template, not an oracle.

**"A no recommendation is unprofessional."** No recommendations are sometimes the most professional output the curriculum director can produce. They require defending a position the proposal on the desk was written to push back against. A reader who can recommend against a tool the budget exists to buy is doing the institution more service than one who rubber-stamps.

**"Renewal will take care of itself."** It will not. Renewal decisions are where SaaS edtech mostly fails, not at initial adoption. The non-renewal triggers in Field 5 are the chapter's most underemphasized field. Year one is the easy decision. Year two is where the institution discovers whether the willingness to act was real.

**"The budget committee wants to hear the full story."** It wants the recommendation first and the supporting structure second. Three sentences and one question. The framework is in the artifact they read after the meeting. The pitch is what they remember in the meeting.

**"If we get this wrong, we are stuck."** Not if the contract is one-year initial with renewal keyed to Field 4 outcomes. The reader is not buying a permanent system. She is buying a one-year test against criteria she set in advance. The non-renewal off-ramp is the structural answer to the worry.

## What would change my mind

A reader who has worked through this chapter may reasonably ask under what conditions the decision framework itself would shift.

Two conditions. First, if the GLP signal evidence base were to accumulate across many deployments to a point where it became settled that the seven friction signals produce robust learning gains over a wide range of institutional contexts, the Q1 scale threshold from Chapter 9 might soften enough that smaller deployments could justify adoption. The framework would still require Q3 (willingness to act) as a hard condition, but the Q1 threshold would shift down. There is theoretical support for the signal evidence; there is not yet enough deployment evidence to settle this question one way or another. Second, if institutional procurement processes for AI-enabled tools became standardized enough that the eight-to-sixteen-week parallel review track from Chapter 10 compressed to four weeks, the timeline considerations in Field 4 would change. Neither has happened yet. Either could, and if either does, the framework gets revised. The reader who finishes this book should be willing to revise her own recommendation when the evidence changes.

## Still puzzling

- The decision tool treats the three-question test as the primary input. It does not address what happens when two questions are unambiguous yes and the third is genuinely contingent on a future event (leadership transition, accreditation cycle, enrollment growth). Should the contingent case have its own template field?
- The non-renewal triggers in Field 5 are measurable. The willingness-to-act condition in Field 2 is harder to make measurable. What is the right calibration for "the institution acted on the data" — one redesign in one term, one redesign per term, a percentage of flagged sections addressed?
- The two-minute pitch assumes a budget committee that asks questions and a curriculum director who has the floor. In garbage-can institutional reality, the committee may not ask the right question, or the director may not have two minutes. What is the half-minute version, when the meeting is running over and the chair calls for votes?
- The model-risk consideration — the underlying foundation model that powers Medhavy's Ask AI and case facilitation will change underneath the deployment over years — is not in the current decision tool. Should it be Field 7? Or is it captured implicitly by Field 5's non-renewal triggers?
