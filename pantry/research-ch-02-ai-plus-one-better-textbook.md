# Research: Chapter 02 — AI+1 Is a Better Textbook. Is That Enough?
## Medhavy: Is the Loop Worth It?

**Chapter one-line:** The reader learns what AI+1 is, what it adds to a Kindle or Canvas deployment, and the specific condition under which that is not sufficient.
**Research date:** 2026-05-27

---

## 1. Primary Sources

### Foundational papers and texts

- **Daugherty, P. R., & Wilson, H. J. (2018).** *Human + Machine: Reimagining Work in the Age of AI.* Harvard Business Review Press. The most readable, decision-maker-facing statement of the "AI augments humans, doesn't replace them" framing. Useful as the precedent the AI+1 framework extends into a *publishing* context. The book frames the "missing middle" — collaboration zones where humans and AI each do what the other cannot — which is the operational ancestor of AI+1's "domain expert plus AI fluency" stance.

- **Bloom, B. S. (1984).** "The 2 Sigma Problem: The Search for Methods of Group Instruction as Effective as One-to-One Tutoring." *Educational Researcher*, 13(6), 4–16. The canonical citation for "personalized instruction works two standard deviations better than classroom instruction." Bloom's tutored students outperformed 98% of conventionally taught students. Relevant here because AI+1 is one *kind* of answer to Bloom's challenge — a textbook written so it sounds like a single expert practitioner explaining to a single reader — but a better-textbook answer, not a measurement answer. The thesis turn of the chapter sits exactly here: Bloom's two-sigma is not closed by improving the textbook alone.

- **Wieman, C. (2014).** "Large-scale comparison of science teaching methods sends clear message." *PNAS*, 111(23), 8319–8320 (Wieman commentary on Freeman et al.). Freeman et al. (2014) meta-analyzed 225 studies of STEM teaching; active learning reduced failure rates from 34% to 22%. Use as the canonical "active learning beats lecture" reference. Important here because AI+1 is a *passive* artifact (a textbook), so the active-learning literature is what argues for the *other* layers in the stack — discussion, Quiz Me, Case Study — that turn passive content into active practice.

- **Bruner, J. S. (1966).** *Toward a Theory of Instruction.* Harvard University Press. Bruner's three core ideas — readiness (you can teach any topic effectively at any age in some intellectually honest form), spiral curriculum (revisit at higher levels), and scaffolding — are the pedagogical backbone underneath the AI+1 framework's claim that domain expertise can be made accessible without dumbing it down. Use Bruner sparingly; reader is not a researcher, but Bruner's framing is the answer to "how can a textbook scale expert thinking?"

- **Copeland, D. B. (2014).** *Brutalist Web Design.* brutalist-web.design (manifesto site). The visual register of the AI+1 stack. Copeland's principles: content readable on all devices; only hyperlinks and buttons respond to clicks; performance is a feature; decoration only when needed. This is *not* an instructional theory — it is a visual-design ethos that aligns with the AI+1 publishing aesthetic. Worth surfacing so the reader understands what "Brutalist slides" means in the deployment stack.

### Key empirical cases

- **Freeman et al. (2014) PNAS active-learning meta-analysis.** 225 studies, STEM courses, n ~ 30,000 students. Active-learning sections had 1.5× higher failure odds in lecture. Documented.

- **Open educational resource (OER) adoption cases (Hilton et al. 2016, *International Review of Research in Open and Distributed Learning*).** Documented institutional cases where moving from a traditional textbook to a free/low-cost OER produced equivalent or improved outcomes. Provides the precedent for the "$1 Kindle is enough" claim — content quality + delivery method can change without learning collapse, *if* the content is good.

- **The nursing-30 vs. multi-section-300 worked example (TIKTOC).** Illustrative; based on common health-sciences class structures. Label as illustrative composite drawn from realistic institutional configurations.

## 2. The Core Concept — State of the Field

### What is settled

- **Active learning beats passive lecture in STEM and health sciences.** Freeman et al. (2014) effect is robust across replications. Not in dispute.
- **Personalized one-to-one tutoring outperforms classroom instruction by a wide margin.** Bloom's two-sigma is occasionally re-litigated (small samples, dissertation work), but the broad shape — tutoring beats group instruction — is uncontested even by skeptics. The argument is over the effect size, not the direction.
- **Textbook quality matters less than how the textbook is used.** Studies of OER and free textbooks consistently find that *delivery method and instructor practice* swamp textbook-quality differences in observed learning outcomes.

### What is disputed

- **Whether "AI+1" is a meaningful category.** This is a proprietary framework from Humanitarians AI; the broader literature has parallel concepts (Daugherty's "missing middle," "human-in-the-loop," "centaur" models in chess and medicine) but no peer-reviewed consensus on AI+1 specifically. Flag in Section 8.
- **Whether *adaptive textbooks* improve outcomes.** Mixed evidence. Several IES-funded studies on adaptive courseware (ALEKS, McGraw-Hill SmartBook) show modest gains; others null. Disputed.
- **Whether the *"better textbook" ceiling* is a real construct.** The book's claim — that a better textbook cannot close Bloom's two-sigma alone, because the missing layer is measurement/adaptation — is plausible but not directly proven by a single study. Treated in the book as a structural argument.

### What has changed recently (last 5 years)

- LLMs enabled *practitioner-voice* textbooks at scale. Pre-2022, a domain-expert textbook required the domain expert to write longform; post-2022, AI+1 collaboration shortened that loop dramatically.
- Kindle / e-reader distribution at $1 became economically viable. The pricing logic of AI+1 (cheap distribution, premium content, no platform lock-in) became standard.
- Canvas added native AI features (Cody, AI Assist) in 2024–2025. The "Canvas + Kindle is enough" claim now requires acknowledging Canvas's own AI feature creep — and explicitly explaining why an LMS-bundled AI is not equivalent to AI+1 content authoring.
- Brutalist design re-emerged as a serious aesthetic in 2023–2025 after a decade of design-system maximalism. The reader may have encountered the term in design press without knowing what it means.

## 3. Application Domain Examples

1. **A pharmacology AI+1 textbook on Kindle.** Written by a hospital pharmacist with LLM-assisted drafting; reads as one practitioner explaining mechanisms, side effects, and clinical edge cases to a student-pharmacist. A class of 30 in one section, one instructor who knows every student — this stack closes the loop *manually*. Three questions: present differently at scale? No (n=30). Need to know whether variation helped? Instructor knows. Willing to act? She's already doing it. Kindle is enough.

2. **A nursing pharmacology course across five sections, 300 students.** Same AI+1 textbook. Three instructors. No shared visibility into which students are struggling with which drug classes. Same content, different deployment context — and here the loop cannot be closed manually. The three questions all flip to yes. Medhavy earns its cost here.

3. **A medical-school pharmacology block, three weeks, two faculty.** Mid-size. The chapter's hinge case. Could go either way depending on whether the faculty are willing to act on measurement and whether the variation across student profiles is meaningful enough to warrant adaptation.

4. **Continuing-education modules for practicing nurses (pharmacology refresher).** Adult learners, self-paced, high motivation, varied prior knowledge. AI+1 + Kindle works because the learner is doing the loop herself — the measurement is internal. Medhavy would be overhead.

5. **A pharmacy-tech certificate program at a community college, 400 students across rolling admissions.** Scale, heterogeneity, accountability all present. Three yeses. Medhavy.

## 4. The Book's Thesis Connection

This is the chapter where the thesis turns from negative (Chapter 1: AI without architecture can harm) to positive (Chapter 2: here is the architecture that *might* be enough — and the three-question test that tells you when it isn't).

The chapter installs the three-question sufficiency test, which is the load-bearing structural device of the entire book:
1. **Does the deployment need to present content differently to different learners at scale?**
2. **Does someone need to know whether the variation is helping?**
3. **Is the institution willing to act on what the measurement reveals?**

Every subsequent chapter is calibrated against this test. The four-mode chapters (3–5) explain *what* the loop presents differently. Chapters 7–9 explain *how* the loop measures whether it helped. Chapters 10–12 walk the reader through *applying* the three-question test to her own deployment.

The chapter's most important job: making the case for the simpler stack *as the right answer* in most deployments — without making Medhavy sound optional in the deployments where it is right. This is the credibility hinge. If the reader finishes Chapter 2 thinking "the author would tell me not to buy this if I shouldn't," she will trust the rest of the book. If she finishes thinking "this is a setup to sell me Medhavy," she will not.

## 6. Pedagogical Delivery Research

**Prior knowledge required:** Some familiarity with Kindle or e-readers, some sense that there is a difference between a "textbook" and an "LMS," and the concept of *scale* in instruction (one section vs. many).

**Common misconceptions in institutional decision-makers:**
1. *"A good AI textbook is an intelligent textbook."* No. AI-authored is not the same as AI-running. AI+1 books are produced with AI; they do not run AI at read time.
2. *"AI+1 already does what Medhavy does."* This is misconception #4 from the TIKTOC's learner profile. The chapter has to install the AI+1-vs-Medhavy distinction explicitly: AI+1 is content; Medhavy is a measurement/adaptation layer.
3. *"More features = better outcomes."* Misconception #5. The chapter must teach the reader to look at her deployment first and the feature list second.
4. *"Adaptive textbooks are AI+1."* Confuses ALEKS-style adaptive courseware with AI+1's practitioner-voice text. The distinction is whether the *content* adapts (adaptive courseware) vs. whether the *content was written by a domain expert with AI assistance and then frozen* (AI+1).

**What teaching of this concept fails at:** The three-question test is often *taught* as a checklist and *understood* as a recipe. Readers want the test to give them an answer. The chapter's job is to teach the reader that the three questions are *honest prompts*, not gates. Her answers should be uncertain in places; the uncertainty is where the work of the rest of the book begins.

**What makes the difference between understanding and memorization:** The reader who can run *someone else's* deployment through the three questions and produce a defensible recommendation has the concept. The reader who can only run her own deployment through the questions has memorized.

## 7. Representation and Display Research

**Opening case:** A short paragraph describing the AI+1 stack — Kindle + Brutalist slides + Canvas. Followed by a paragraph naming what the stack is *not*: not a platform, not a measurement system, not adaptive. The reader needs to see the absence as a feature, not a gap.

**Worked example:** The two-deployment side-by-side from the TIKTOC (30 nursing / 300 multi-section). Recommend a **two-column comparison table** running down the page:

| Dimension | Deployment A (Kindle is enough) | Deployment B (Medhavy earns its cost) |
|---|---|---|
| Class size | 30 | 300 across 5 sections |
| Instructors | 1, knows every student | 3, no shared visibility |
| Heterogeneity | Low — single cohort | High — multiple pathways into the program |
| Adapts in real time | Yes — instructor | No — no human can see at this scale |
| Three-question answers | No / No / Maybe | Yes / Yes / Yes |
| Right tool | AI+1 + Kindle + Canvas | AI+1 + Kindle + Canvas + Medhavy |

**Display required:** the three-question test as a small standalone box, repeated at the chapter close so the reader leaves the chapter with the questions in working memory. This is the load-bearing artifact for the rest of the book.

## 8. Open Questions and Research Gaps

- **AI+1 as a proprietary framework.** Humanitarians AI's specific framing of "AI+1" and "Irreducibly Human" will not appear in PubMed or ERIC. Cite the Humanitarians AI manuscripts as the primary source; place the framework in the broader Daugherty/Licklider/centaur-model lineage so the reader understands it is a *naming* of a recognizable idea, not an invented one.
- **"Irreducibly Human" taxonomy.** Proprietary. Flag.
- **The three-question sufficiency test.** Internal to this book; not externally validated. Frame as a decision-aid heuristic, not a research-derived instrument.
- **Brutalist slides as a category.** Outside-of-Medhavy primary sources for *Brutalist slides specifically* (vs. Brutalist web design) are thin. The connection is by analogy. Flag.
- **Whether AI+1 textbooks generalize across domains.** The pharmacology case is well-suited; whether nursing fundamentals, medical ethics, or anatomy benefit equally is open. Frame health sciences as an exemplar, not a universal.
- **Aging risk:** Canvas's AI features will continue to expand. By 2027, "Canvas + Kindle" may include LMS-side AI that closes part of the gap Medhavy currently addresses. Chapter should be written so the *category distinction* (content vs. measurement layer) survives feature changes.
