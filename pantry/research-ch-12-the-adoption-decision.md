# Research: Chapter 12 — The Adoption Decision
## Medhavy: Is the Loop Worth It?

**Chapter one-line:** The reader produces their justified adoption recommendation — for or against, with specific reasoning — and knows what success looks like if the answer is yes.
**Research date:** 2026-05-27

---

## 1. Primary Sources

### Foundational papers and texts
- **Klein, G. (1998).** *Sources of Power: How People Make Decisions.* MIT Press. Klein's Recognition-Primed Decision (RPD) model is built on field studies of how experienced decision-makers — firefighters, ICU nurses, military commanders — actually decide under uncertainty. They do not weigh options against multi-criteria matrices. They recognize the situation as a familiar pattern, mentally simulate one course of action, and proceed if the simulation produces an acceptable outcome. Direct relevance: the curriculum director is an experienced decision-maker. The book's job is not to make her decision for her — it is to give her the pattern-recognition vocabulary she does not yet have for Medhavy specifically. Once she has it, RPD takes over.
- **Christensen, C. M. (1997, 2003).** *The Innovator's Dilemma* and *The Innovator's Solution*. The original framework for when an incumbent technology is good enough and when it is not. Direct mapping: AI+1 on Kindle is the incumbent that is good enough for most jobs; Medhavy is the new entrant that earns its place only where the incumbent cannot do the job (scale + measurement + adaptation). Christensen's "job to be done" framing is the cleanest way to articulate when adoption is correct.
- **Birnbaum, R. (1988).** *How Colleges Work: The Cybernetics of Academic Organization and Leadership.* Jossey-Bass. The classic on HE institutional decision-making. The "garbage can model" applied to academic budgets — decisions get made when problems, solutions, participants, and choice opportunities collide, not in a tidy rational process. The book's two-minute pitch advice must be calibrated against this reality: the reader is not presenting to optimizers, she is presenting to a coalition with mixed priorities and limited attention.
- **Tierney, W. G. (1993).** *Building Communities of Difference: Higher Education in the Twenty-First Century.* SUNY Press. On how HE institutions negotiate change across constituencies. Less directly relevant than Birnbaum but useful for the chapter's framing of the budget committee as a community of constituencies, not a board of fiduciaries.
- **Doctorow, C. (2023).** *The Internet Con: How to Seize the Means of Computation.* Verso. The contemporary "when not to buy" argument for SaaS in general. Doctorow's "enshittification" frame — platforms get worse over time because their incentives shift — is a real consideration the curriculum director should be aware of when evaluating a SaaS edtech tool with long curricular commitments. Particularly relevant to the renewal/non-renewal section of the chapter.
- **Stoll, C. (1995).** *Silicon Snake Oil.* The original "when not to buy" book for educational technology. Older but still useful as the historical anchor — every technological wave produces a wave of overpromising; the skeptical voice is part of the canon.
- **Sinek, S. (2009).** *Start With Why.* The "golden circle" — Why → How → What. Useful but limited. Better executive-briefing framework for the two-minute pitch:
- **Minto, B. (1987).** *The Pyramid Principle.* The McKinsey standard. Argues that executive communication should be top-down: the bottom-line conclusion first (BLOT — Bottom Line On Top), then the supporting arguments, then the data. The two-minute budget-committee pitch must follow this structure, not the chronological "let me explain what Medhavy is" structure.
- **Heath, C. & Heath, D. (2007).** *Made to Stick* and (2013) *Decisive*. *Decisive* is the more relevant text for Ch 11 — the WRAP framework (Widen options, Reality-test assumptions, Attain distance, Prepare to be wrong) is the procedural backstop for any adoption decision. The chapter's "what would cause you to not renew" exercise is the "Prepare to be wrong" step.
- **Hatch, M. J. & Cunliffe, A. L. (2013).** *Organization Theory* (3rd ed.). Oxford. The synthesis-chapter genre exemplar — how a final chapter ties together the conceptual architecture of an entire book without summarizing it. The Hatch & Cunliffe final chapter is a good structural model for Ch 11 because it gives the reader an integrated framework rather than a recap.
- **Mehta, N., Steinman, D. & Murphy, L. (2016).** *Customer Success: How Innovative Companies Are Reducing Churn and Growing Recurring Revenue.* Wiley. The SaaS literature on what renewal/non-renewal looks like from the vendor side. Relevant inversion for the chapter: the reader will be the vendor's customer-success target. Knowing the playbook lets her set her own renewal criteria honestly.
- **Reichheld, F. & Markey, R. (2011).** *The Ultimate Question 2.0: How Net Promoter Companies Thrive in a Customer-Driven World.* Bain. The Net Promoter Score literature. Less central but useful for the "is the user community thriving" check.
- **EDUCAUSE Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) frameworks for edtech.** Various ECAR studies, especially the 2021–2023 cost-of-tech surveys. The reader's TCO for Medhavy is not just the license fee — it is license + faculty time + instructional design + ongoing curricular maintenance. Gartner's general SaaS TCO framework (license × 3–5 = realistic TCO over a deployment lifetime) is a useful rule-of-thumb.

### Key empirical cases
- **Carnegie Mellon's Open Learning Initiative (OLI).** The closest analog to a genuinely-adaptive university platform with longitudinal outcome data. Showed real learning gains (the "Accelerated Learning" study with statistics) but required intensive curricular labor. The pattern Medhavy adoption will likely repeat where it succeeds.
- **ALEKS at community colleges.** Documented gains in developmental mathematics; documented institutional struggles when the surrounding curriculum did not align. Useful as the cautionary case the curriculum director's committee may know.
- **Khanmigo in K-12 districts (2023–2025).** Mixed adoption results: technical integration smooth, instructor uptake variable, learning outcomes pending. Too new for definitive judgment; useful as "this is the current landscape" anchor.

## 2. The Core Concept — State of the Field

### What is settled
- Institutional adoption decisions in HE are not made by individuals; they are made by committees with mixed priorities (Birnbaum). The book's job is to equip the individual reader to advocate effectively in that committee.
- Total cost of ownership for SaaS edtech tools in HE substantially exceeds the license fee, typically by 3–5x once faculty time, instructional design, and curricular maintenance are counted (EDUCAUSE / Gartner / multiple institutional case studies).
- Executive-briefing structure (bottom line first, supporting evidence second, detail on request) outperforms chronological explanation for time-constrained audiences (Minto; multiple McKinsey communication papers).
- Renewal decisions, not initial adoption decisions, are where the long-term ROI gets determined. First-term adoption is the easy decision; year-two renewal is the honest one.

### What is disputed
- Whether "adaptive learning" platforms produce population-level gains that justify cost (the Pane et al. 2014 RAND study found modest gains for Cognitive Tutor; subsequent meta-analyses have been more cautious). Medhavy is in this category. The chapter cannot claim a settled positive finding; it can claim a defensible theoretical case with first-term measurement criteria.
- Whether a decision-framework book itself improves decisions — i.e., whether the reader who reads Ch 11 actually decides better than the reader who does not. Limited empirical evidence in either direction. The chapter should be honest about this: it offers structure, not a guarantee.

### What has changed recently (last 5 years)
- Generative AI has made every existing edtech procurement process a moving target. Tools that were evaluated in 2022 are functionally different in 2026. Institutional procurement is under pressure to be faster; security reviews are under pressure to be deeper. The chapter should acknowledge this tension.
- The "AI hype cycle" has produced widespread vendor-fatigue in HE. The curriculum director's committee is more skeptical than they were three years ago. The two-minute pitch must respect that skepticism, not paper over it.
- TCO frameworks for AI-enabled SaaS now include "model risk" (the underlying foundation model changes, costs rise, capabilities shift) as a new line item. Educause first added this to their TCO templates in 2024.

## 3. Application Domain Examples

1. **Pharmacology, 4 sections × 80 students, board-pass pressure.** The recommendation the book is building toward. The reader writes: "Recommend adoption. All three conditions hold. First-term success = closing 50% of the inter-section pass-rate gap. Non-renewal trigger = no measurable narrowing of the gap by end of term 2."

2. **Nursing fundamentals, single section of 28.** The recommendation against. The reader writes: "Recommend against. Question 1 fails — scale insufficient for the loop to add value. Revisit if the program expands to 3+ sections or the lead instructor leaves."

3. **Pre-clinical clerkship, 240 students × 8 sites.** Recommend conditionally. Adoption depends on the clerkship committee's pre-commitment to act on data — a specific governance step that should happen before the contract is signed, not after.

4. **Pharmacy CE modules, ~500/quarter.** Recommend against. The reward signal is wrong — completion is what the regulator counts; durable learning is what Medhavy measures. The mismatch makes Medhavy expensive overhead.

5. **Anatomy & physiology, two sections × 60, two non-coordinating instructors.** Recommend against — for now. The barrier is institutional (instructors will not coordinate), not technological. Revisit when departmental leadership changes or when accreditation pressure forces coordination.

The chapter should run all five through the decision tool in fast succession, showing that the test does not always say yes — and does not always say no. The variation is the point.

## 4. The Book's Thesis Connection

Chapter 12 is the chapter that delivers what the book promised in its title: the reader can answer "Is the loop worth it?" for her own deployment.

The thesis-relevant work is the **integration** step. Chapters 1–9 built the conceptual machinery (modes, signals, loop). Chapter 10 named the conditions under which the machinery does not apply. Chapter 11 specified the institutional cost of running the machinery. Chapter 12's job is to bring all of this into a single justified recommendation that the reader can defend in a budget meeting.

The chapter must also enforce the book's anti-vendor positioning. The reader has been told eight chapters' worth of why Medhavy is theoretically interesting and three chapters' worth of when it is wrong. The final chapter must close the loop on the book's central commitment: "this book tells you exactly when it isn't" (back-cover copy from the TIKTOC).

The "two-minute pitch" exercise is the chapter's most reader-facing artifact. It instantiates the book's voice — the trusted colleague — by giving the reader the exact words she can use, not the abstract framework. A reader who finishes Ch 11 with a one-page recommendation and a two-minute pitch in hand has gotten what the book promised. A reader who finishes with neither has not.

The chapter must also resolve the "what does success look like" question with honest specificity. Success is not "the GLP scores improved." Success is "the inter-section pass-rate gap narrowed by X% over Y terms." The book's credibility — and Medhavy's credibility — depends on the success criterion being concrete enough to fail.

## 5. The AI Wayback Machine — Candidate Figures

**Recommended: Gerd Gigerenzer.** German psychologist, Director Emeritus of the Max Planck Institute for Human Development. Author of *Risk Savvy* (2014), *Gut Feelings* (2007), *Rationality for Mortals* (2008). Gigerenzer's central work is on "fast and frugal heuristics" — decision rules that perform well under uncertainty without requiring exhaustive analysis. The three-question test is precisely a fast-and-frugal heuristic. Diversity scrutiny: non-Anglo (German), male, well-credentialed but accessible. Not as famous as Kahneman, more useful than Kahneman for this chapter because his work is explicitly about decisions under uncertainty by non-experts, not lab subjects. **Use him.**

**Strong alternative: Mary Parker Follett (1868–1933).** Early management theorist; her *Creative Experience* (1924) and lectures collected in *Dynamic Administration* (1941, posthumous) are extraordinary on integrative decision-making — finding decisions that satisfy multiple constituencies rather than compromising between them. Anti-zero-sum framing. Woman, early-20th-century, neglected. Diversity scrutiny: woman, historical, white, US-based but writing about labor relations and pluralism in ways far ahead of her era. The chapter could use her as the "integrative decision" anchor — the budget committee is exactly the constituency-mediation problem she described.

**Honorable mention: Karen Stephenson.** Corporate anthropologist, network analyst. Her work on "trust networks" inside organizations is directly relevant to who actually drives adoption decisions (rarely the formal authority). Less central than Gigerenzer for the decision-procedure question; more central for the "talking to the committee" sub-section.

**Avoid:** Daniel Kahneman (too famous, Ch 1 / Ch 6 risk of overuse), Herbert Simon (canonical but very academic — accessibility concern).

For Ch 11 final: Gigerenzer is the right primary. Follett can appear in the talking-to-the-committee section.

## 6. Pedagogical Delivery Research

The chapter must do four pedagogical things:

1. **Give the reader a one-page decision tool she will actually use.** Format question is the open question (see Section 7 and TIKTOC Open Question 6).
2. **Show the tool being used.** The worked example must be a recommendation written in the voice of the book's primary reader, not the book's voice. The reader should be able to imagine using these words.
3. **Equip the two-minute pitch.** Three sentences. No jargon. Must answer: what does Medhavy do that Canvas doesn't, when is it not the right answer.
4. **Name the failure modes.** The three documented failure patterns from the chapter outline: wrong deployment context, insufficient curriculum design, unwillingness to act. Each gets one named example.

The chapter should *not* end with motivational summary. It should end with the closing paragraph from the TIKTOC: "AI+1 is a better textbook. For most deployments, that is enough. For deployments that need... Medhavy closes the loop." That closing is doing real work — it returns the reader to the book's thesis and grants the reader the authority to decide.

## 7. Representation and Display Research

**The chapter's most important display question (TIKTOC Open Question 6): is the one-page decision tool a table, a flowchart, or prose?**

Each format has costs:

**Table.** Pros: scannable, fits one Kindle page, can be photocopied to a budget-meeting handout, fields are obvious. Cons: implies more deterministic decision-making than the chapter intends; the reader's nuance gets lost in a yes/no cell.

**Flowchart.** Pros: shows decision logic explicitly, including the partial cases (one-yes, two-yes). Cons: hard to read on Kindle, hard to use as a planning artifact, can feel mechanical for a decision that should be reflective.

**Prose recommendation template.** Pros: forces the reader to think rather than tick boxes, produces an artifact the budget committee can actually read, matches the "trusted colleague" voice of the book. Cons: less scannable, less obviously "the tool."

**Recommendation: Hybrid — structured template (prose with fixed fields).** Six fields the reader fills in:
1. **Deployment under evaluation** (1 sentence)
2. **Three-question answers and reasoning** (3 short paragraphs, one per question)
3. **What Medhavy would add that Canvas does not** (2–3 sentences, jargon-free)
4. **First-term success criteria** (measurable, time-bounded — e.g., "section-to-section pass-rate gap narrows by 50% by end of term 2")
5. **Non-renewal triggers** (2–3 specific outcomes that would cause non-renewal)
6. **Honest acknowledgment of uncertainty** (what the reader does not know yet and how she will find out)

The hybrid format respects three things the binary formats do not: that the decision is reflective rather than mechanical, that the budget committee needs to read prose rather than parse a diagram, and that the renewal decision (field 5) is as important as the adoption decision.

A small auxiliary table can sit alongside the template — three rows for the three questions, four columns (deployment A through D) — for readers who want to compare deployments side by side.

This is the highest-priority gap to resolve before drafting. Author input needed.

## 8. Open Questions and Research Gaps

1. **Format of the one-page decision tool (TIKTOC Open Question 6).** Research-strongest recommendation above (hybrid structured template). Final call belongs to the author.
2. **Talking-to-the-budget-committee section: how much vendor-comparison detail?** The chapter has explicitly not compared Medhavy to ALEKS / Khanmigo / Cognitive Tutor anywhere else in the book. Should Ch 11 break that rule when arming the reader for the committee? Author decision.
3. **First-term success criteria — should the chapter give a default set or insist the reader define them locally?** Research suggests local definition; the chapter could offer a worked example set as a starting template.
4. **The non-renewal section may need to be longer than currently outlined.** Renewal decisions are where SaaS edtech mostly fails, not at initial adoption. Worth 200–300 words explicit treatment.
5. **The "model risk" line item** (foundation model changes underneath Medhavy) is a 2024+ concern the book has not addressed elsewhere. Worth a sentence in the TCO section.

## 9. Sourcing Notes

- Klein's RPD model is the deepest theoretical anchor for "experienced decision-makers don't multi-criteria-optimize." Cite once, use the frame throughout without repeated invocation.
- Christensen ("job to be done") is the cleanest articulation of when adoption is correct; the chapter can use the frame implicitly without dwelling on Christensen's name.
- Minto's Pyramid Principle / BLOT is the right reference for the two-minute pitch structure — but the chapter should give the reader the structure, not the source.
- Gigerenzer is the wayback figure; Follett can come in as a secondary voice for the committee-decision section if the chapter has room.
- EDUCAUSE TCO data is the authoritative source for "license × 3–5 = TCO" claims.
- Avoid citing Christensen as a definitive predictor of which technologies will disrupt — his predictive accuracy has been challenged. Use his frame, not his prophetic record.
- Carnegie Mellon OLI is the strongest positive empirical anchor for "adaptive systems can work with sufficient curricular labor." Cite once.
- The "enshittification" / Doctorow frame is sharp and current but politically loaded for some readers. Use the concept (platform incentives shift over time) without the term, unless the chapter wants to be more provocative than its voice currently is.
- The closing paragraph (TIKTOC verbatim) should appear at the chapter's end with at most light editing — it is the book's load-bearing final beat.
