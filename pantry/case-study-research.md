# Case-Based Learning in Medical and Health Sciences Education

*Research note for Medhavy's "Case Study" mode design and the corresponding book chapter. Compiled 2026-05-17. Author: research subagent for Nik Bear Brown.*

> **Reader's compass.** This note distinguishes three traditions that are routinely conflated in EdTech marketing: Problem-Based Learning (PBL), Case-Based Learning (CBL), and Team-Based Learning (TBL). Every effect-size citation below is tagged with which approach it actually evaluates. Where evidence for one approach is being extrapolated to another (e.g., PBL evidence used to justify AI-facilitated case work), the extrapolation is named explicitly.

---

## 0. The three traditions — get this right before anything else

The literature on "case study" pedagogy in health sciences is mostly a literature on three distinct designs with overlapping vocabulary. Conflating them is the single most common error in EdTech claims.

**Problem-Based Learning (PBL).** Originated at McMaster University Medical School in 1969 under Howard Barrows. Small groups (5–8 students) work a single ill-structured case over multiple sessions across a week or longer. No prior preparation expected — students *encounter* the case cold and identify what they need to learn ("learning issues"). A faculty *tutor* facilitates but does not teach content; tutors are often non-experts in the case's clinical domain on purpose. Lectures are minimized or absent. The pedagogical theory is constructivist, with cognitive-flexibility and information-processing roots (Barrows 1986; Schmidt 1983; Norman and Schmidt 1992).

**Case-Based Learning (CBL).** A guided-inquiry derivative. Students *prepare in advance* using readings or pre-work. A faculty facilitator runs a session — often larger than a PBL group (10–30 students) — built around specific learning objectives. The case typically has a defined endpoint and "best" management decisions. Faculty are expected to be content experts. Sessions are shorter (60–120 minutes) and cycle faster (often weekly). This is what most US medical schools mean today when they say "case-based" (Srinivasan et al. 2007; Thistlethwaite et al. 2012; McLean 2016).

**Team-Based Learning (TBL).** Larry Michaelsen's design, developed in business education in the 1970s–80s and ported to medical education in the early 2000s. Students prepare, then take an Individual Readiness Assurance Test (iRAT), then the same test as a team (tRAT), with appeals, then work application exercises around a case. Highly structured, instructor-led, designed for one expert managing many small teams in a single large room (Michaelsen, Knight, and Fink 2004; Parmelee et al. 2012).

**Why the distinction matters for Medhavy.** Effect sizes do not transfer across these designs. The Dochy et al. (2003) meta-analysis effect for PBL on skills (+0.46 SD) cannot be cited as evidence that a CBL-style scenario produces equivalent gains, and neither can be cited as evidence for AI-facilitated case discussion. The traditions differ in preparation expectations, group size, facilitator role, time scale, and what "the answer" looks like at the end. Borrowing evidence across them is the same category error as citing aspirin RCTs to justify ibuprofen dosing.

## 1. Effectiveness evidence

### 1.1 The canonical CBL synthesis — Thistlethwaite et al. 2012 [CBL]

Thistlethwaite, Davies, Ekeocha, Kidd, MacDougall, Matthews, Purkis, and Clay (2012), *The Effectiveness of Case-Based Learning in Health Professional Education* — BEME Guide No. 23 — is the canonical synthesis. Published in *Medical Teacher* 34(6): e421–e444.

- **Design:** Systematic review under Best Evidence Medical Education (BEME) Collaboration protocols. 104 studies retained after screening, of which 41 met inclusion criteria for analysis (104 cited, 41 fully analyzed). Studies spanned medical, dental, nursing, pharmacy, and allied health programs.
- **Sample characteristics:** Mostly undergraduate health professional education; cohort sizes from <50 to several hundred; comparators heterogeneous (lecture, PBL, traditional curriculum).
- **Key findings (verbatim with effect direction, not magnitude — the review is mostly qualitative because the underlying studies are too heterogeneous to meta-analyze):**
  - Students and faculty *prefer* CBL to lecture-only formats.
  - Students perceive CBL as *enjoyable*, *engaging*, and as *promoting clinical reasoning*.
  - Knowledge acquisition is *not consistently superior* to other formats — equivalence is the modal finding.
  - Evidence for *long-term retention* and *transfer to clinical practice* is "limited and inconsistent."
  - The review explicitly notes that CBL is *not the same as PBL* and that the literatures should not be merged.
- **The honest takeaway:** CBL produces student satisfaction and self-reported clinical reasoning gains. It does not produce reliably superior knowledge outcomes versus lecture in head-to-head comparisons. The 2012 review is the strongest single document Medhavy should quarry, and its core verdict is "CBL works about as well as lecture for facts, possibly better for application, students like it more, the evidence base remains thin for hard outcomes."

### 1.2 The foundational PBL papers — Norman and Schmidt 1992, 2000 [PBL]

Norman and Schmidt (1992), "The Psychological Basis of Problem-Based Learning: A Review of the Evidence," *Academic Medicine* 67(9): 557–565. Updated in Norman and Schmidt (2000), "Effectiveness of Problem-Based Learning Curricula: Theory, Practice and Paper Darts," *Medical Education* 34(9): 721–728.

- **The original argument:** PBL should produce three effects relative to lecture — better retention (because of elaboration during the tutorial), better transfer (because of context-rich encoding), and better self-directed learning skills.
- **The 1992 verdict:** Evidence for *retention* was weak. Evidence for *transfer* was modestly supportive. Evidence for *self-directed learning* was supportive but underpowered.
- **The 2000 verdict (with eight more years of data):** Schmidt and Norman walked back some of the strongest early claims. PBL graduates do not score better on standardized medical knowledge exams (USMLE Step 1 effects are null or trivially small). PBL graduates *do* score modestly better on clinical performance assessments and report higher satisfaction. The "paper darts" subtitle is a deliberate jab at how cleanly the early PBL claims had been weaponized in curriculum politics.
- **What this means for Medhavy:** PBL evidence does *not* support claims like "case-based pedagogy produces better knowledge outcomes." It supports a narrower claim: case-based pedagogy may produce modestly better clinical-reasoning-flavored skill outcomes and reliably better student-reported engagement.

### 1.3 The PBL meta-analysis — Dochy et al. 2003 [PBL]

Dochy, Segers, Van den Bossche, and Gijbels (2003), "Effects of Problem-Based Learning: A Meta-Analysis," *Learning and Instruction* 13(5): 533–568.

- **Design:** Meta-analysis of 43 empirical studies of PBL in higher education (medical and non-medical).
- **Headline effect sizes:**
  - PBL on **knowledge acquisition:** *negative* effect (approximately −0.22 SD), meaning PBL students score *slightly worse* on knowledge tests than lecture controls. The effect is small but consistent.
  - PBL on **skills application:** *positive* effect (approximately +0.46 SD), meaning PBL students perform meaningfully better on skill-application tasks.
- **Interpretation:** PBL trades knowledge breadth for application depth. This is consistent with the cognitive-flexibility theory rationale — PBL students encode in fewer contexts but with richer elaboration. Whether the trade is worth it depends on what the curriculum is for. A licensing exam that tests recall would be hurt by full PBL; a clinical clerkship that tests application would benefit.
- **For Medhavy:** This is the cleanest quantitative finding in the literature. It supports the claim that case-based work *complements* rather than *replaces* knowledge-acquisition activities. Medhavy's design — Case Study as one of four modes, used *after* the representation exists — is consistent with the Dochy finding, not in tension with it.

### 1.4 Comparative CBL studies — Williams 2005, Tarnvik 2007, McLean 2016 [CBL]

- **Williams (2005),** "Case Based Learning — A Review of the Literature: Is There Scope for This Educational Paradigm in Prehospital Education?" *Emergency Medicine Journal* 22(8): 577–581. A narrative review concluding that CBL "appears to be a useful pedagogical approach" but that "robust outcome data is sparse." Notable for its caution about overclaiming.
- **Tarnvik (2007),** "Revival of the Case Method: A Way to Retain Student-Centred Learning in a Post-PBL Era," *Medical Teacher* 29(1): e32–e36. Argues that many medical schools that adopted PBL in the 1990s reverted toward CBL because the resource cost of PBL (one tutor per 6–8 students for week-long cases) was unsustainable. The case method is presented as the politically viable compromise: keeps the constructivist flavor, fits a lecture-hall schedule, requires fewer expert facilitators.
- **McLean (2016),** "Case-Based Learning and Its Application in Medical and Health-Care Fields: A Review of Worldwide Literature," *Journal of Medical Education and Curricular Development* 3 (open access — note: this is *JMECD*, not the larger journal the prompt named; *Advances in Medical Education and Practice* did not publish this McLean review; the McLean paper that does exist is in *JMECD*). The review surveys CBL across medicine, nursing, pharmacy, and dentistry. Conclusions echo Thistlethwaite: students prefer it, faculty find it sustainable, hard outcome evidence remains limited.

### 1.5 PBL and clinical reasoning — Hartling et al. 2010 [PBL]

The prompt cited "Hartling et al. 2010 (*JAMA*) on PBL in clinical reasoning." The Hartling group has published systematic reviews on PBL but the specific *JAMA* citation does not resolve cleanly to a single paper in my reconstruction — Hartling, Spooner, Tjosvold, and Oswald (2010), "Problem-Based Learning in Pre-Clinical Medical Education: 22 Years of Outcome Research," *Medical Teacher* 32(1): 28–35 is the most likely intended reference. `[verify]`

- **Findings (Medical Teacher version):** A systematic review of PBL outcome studies 1988–2010. Concluded that PBL effects on knowledge are *small and inconsistent*, effects on clinical performance are *positive but modest*, and effects on student satisfaction are *robust and large*. Mirrors the Norman/Schmidt 2000 walk-back.

### 1.6 Effect sizes consolidated, by approach

| Outcome | PBL effect | CBL effect | TBL effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Knowledge (recall) | ≈ −0.2 SD (Dochy) | ≈ 0 (Thistlethwaite) | ≈ +0.1–0.3 SD (small-N TBL studies, e.g., Koles et al. 2010) |
| Skills / application | ≈ +0.46 SD (Dochy) | Equivocal — favorable trend (Thistlethwaite) | Equivocal — favorable trend (Parmelee et al. 2012) |
| Long-term retention | Weak evidence | Insufficient evidence | Insufficient evidence |
| Clinical reasoning (assessed) | +modest (Hartling) | Self-reported gains, weak objective evidence | Insufficient evidence |
| Student satisfaction | Large positive | Large positive | Large positive |

The pattern across all three approaches is the same: large self-report effects, modest skill-application effects, null-to-negative knowledge effects, thin long-term evidence. Anyone claiming a large knowledge gain from case-based pedagogy of any flavor is overclaiming.

### 1.7 AI-facilitated vs. instructor-facilitated case discussion [AI-facilitated]

**Direct comparative RCTs of AI-facilitated vs. faculty-facilitated case discussion in medical education: essentially none as of the cutoff date.** This is the most important negative finding in this research note.

What exists is adjacent:

- **NEJM AI Healer / Aquifer cases:** vendor-published descriptive evidence of student usage and satisfaction. Not independent RCTs. Labeled vendor source. [vendor]
- **Khanmigo in K–12 contexts:** Khan Academy and partners (e.g., Newark Public Schools pilots reported through Khan's own materials and through Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation grant reports) have published engagement data. The IDK IDK finding (Kristen DiCerbo, Khan Academy, public remarks 2024–2025) is itself a *vendor disclosure of failure mode*, not an independent evaluation. [vendor]
- **GPT-4 as Socratic tutor in problem-solving (not clinical case discussion):** Bastani et al. (2025), "Generative AI Can Harm Learning," *PNAS* — see §4. This study used math problem-solving, not clinical case discussion. The finding is suggestive but not direct evidence for case work.
- **Med-PaLM and Med-PaLM 2 evaluations** (Singhal et al. 2023): test the LLM's *own* clinical reasoning, not the LLM's ability to *facilitate* a student's clinical reasoning. Different question.

The literature available to underwrite "AI as case facilitator" claims is the literature on AI tutors in general — which is mostly K–12, mostly math and writing, and mostly evaluated on engagement rather than transfer. Any chapter Medhavy writes on this should say so directly.

### 1.8 Pre-written vs. on-demand AI-generated cases — the Medhavy-specific question

**The empirical answer: there are no head-to-head RCTs comparing instructor-prepared cases against AI-generated-on-demand cases in medical education.** What exists is indirect evidence pointing toward a clear precaution.

- **Aleven and Koedinger (2002), "An Effective Metacognitive Strategy: Learning by Doing and Explaining with a Computer-Based Cognitive Tutor,"** *Cognitive Science* 26(2): 147–179 — the foundational Cognitive Tutor work. The Carnegie Learning / ALEKS / Cognitive Tutor tradition relies on *carefully authored* problem banks with validated solution paths. The empirical record (positive effects on math achievement in multiple RCTs, e.g., Pane et al. 2014, *Journal of Research on Educational Effectiveness*) is built on *curated content*, not generated content. [foundational]
- **Authoring cost is the central trade-off.** Koedinger and colleagues consistently report 100–300 hours of expert authoring per hour of student-facing tutor content. Medhavy's design implicitly proposes generation as the workaround. The empirical record for the workaround is absent.
- **The hallucination risk is asymmetric.** A hallucinated arithmetic step in a math tutor is recoverable — the student or facilitator catches it. A hallucinated clinical guideline ("first-line treatment for X is Y") in a case scenario is a teaching error that propagates as a misconception. The asymmetry argues for human review of every clinical case, not for on-demand generation. (See §3 on currency and review.)
- **What the literature *does* support:** AI as a *case author's assistant* under human review (a hybrid model), and AI as a *facilitator* of pre-authored cases. Both preserve the human review gate. Neither is "on-demand generation."

**The strongest empirical answer available: pre-written cases under faculty review are the design choice consistent with the existing evidence base; on-demand AI-generated cases sit outside that evidence base and inherit the hallucination risk documented in the medical LLM literature (Singhal et al. 2023; Lee et al. 2023). The burden of evidence is on the on-demand design to show it doesn't propagate clinical errors as learning content.**

This is the cleanest reason Medhavy's "professor-approved scenarios" framing is the right design choice. Not because pre-written is provably better — the comparison hasn't been run — but because the failure modes of the alternative are documented and the failure modes of pre-written are bounded by the same review processes medical education has used for decades.

## 2. Design principles for effective cases

### 2.1 Ill-structured problems — Jonassen and Spiro [foundational]

Jonassen (1997), "Instructional Design Models for Well-Structured and Ill-Structured Problem-Solving Learning Outcomes," *Educational Technology Research and Development* 45(1): 65–94. The canonical taxonomy of problem types.

- **Well-structured problems** have a defined initial state, a defined goal state, a constrained solution path, and rules for evaluating solutions. Most textbook problems.
- **Ill-structured problems** have unclear goals, multiple solution paths, conflicting evidence, and require judgment about which framing to apply. Most professional problems.
- **Implication for case design:** A "case" that has a single right answer hidden behind a narrative is a well-structured problem in disguise. It teaches recognition of the canonical answer, not the judgment under uncertainty that the profession requires. Jonassen's argument is that ill-structured problem-solving is a *different cognitive operation* and requires different instruction.

Spiro et al. (1988, 1991, 2003), the cognitive flexibility theory program. Spiro, Coulson, Feltovich, and Anderson (1988), "Cognitive Flexibility Theory: Advanced Knowledge Acquisition in Ill-Structured Domains," *Proceedings of the 10th Annual Conference of the Cognitive Science Society*; Spiro et al. (1991) in *Educational Technology*; Spiro, Collins, Thota, and Feltovich (2003) in *Educational Technology*.

- **Core claim:** Advanced knowledge in ill-structured domains (medicine, law, history) requires *multiple cases studied from multiple perspectives*. A single canonical case produces overconfident pattern-matching. Multiple cases that share underlying mechanism but differ in surface features produce flexible application.
- **Implication for Medhavy:** Case Study mode should expose the same underlying concept through multiple cases, not the same case through multiple sessions. The case library matters more than any individual case.

### 2.2 What makes a case "genuinely ambiguous"

Drawing from Jonassen, Spiro, and the clinical reasoning literature (Norman 2005; Eva 2005; Bowen 2006), the operational markers of a well-designed ambiguous case are:

1. **Multiple plausible diagnoses or actions consistent with the same evidence.** If the case has one clinically correct answer, it is a vignette, not an ambiguous case.
2. **Information that is incomplete in a way the student must notice.** A case that tells you everything you need to know is testing recall of canonical workups, not clinical reasoning under uncertainty.
3. **Competing legitimate values.** The intern wants to admit; the family wants to go home; the attending wants imaging before deciding; the hospital wants the bed. All four positions are defensible. The "right" answer is the reasoning that names the trade-off, not the verdict.
4. **Red herrings that resolve themselves.** A red herring whose only function is to mislead is bad pedagogy. A red herring that *looks* like the key piece of evidence on first read and reveals itself as irrelevant after careful reasoning is good pedagogy. The latter is how clinical reasoning actually develops (Eva 2005).

### 2.3 Case complexity dimensions

The Newble and Cannon framework — Newble and Cannon (2001), *A Handbook for Medical Teachers*, 4th ed. — names four dimensions of clinical case complexity that case authors should manipulate deliberately:

1. **Information density.** How many distinct facts must be integrated. More facts = harder, but also more noise.
2. **Distractor count.** How many plausible but ultimately incorrect framings the case admits.
3. **Time pressure.** Whether the case is presented as a single static snapshot or as a dynamic situation evolving across hours/days.
4. **Stakes asymmetry.** Whether wrong decisions cost differently — missing a pulmonary embolism costs more than missing a sprain. Asymmetric stakes change reasoning strategy in ways the case should make visible.

Norman (2005), "Research in Clinical Reasoning: Past History and Current Trends," *Medical Education* 39(4): 418–427 — the canonical synthesis. Norman's key empirical point: expert clinicians do *not* reason by hypothetico-deductive method most of the time. They reason by *pattern recognition* (Type 1) on familiar presentations, and switch to *analytic reasoning* (Type 2) on unfamiliar ones. The case design implication is that good cases should require *both* — recognition of a pattern, then a twist that forces Type 2 reasoning. This is the bread-and-butter of well-designed clinical cases.

### 2.4 Open vs. closed cases

The literature is genuinely divided on whether cases should converge to a "correct answer."

- **Closed-case position (e.g., AAMC MedEdPORTAL standards):** Cases need defined learning objectives, defined "best" management, defined feedback. Otherwise the facilitator can't grade, the student doesn't know what to take away, and the case fails to teach.
- **Open-case position (Spiro, Jonassen, the PBL tradition):** Converging on a single answer teaches the wrong cognitive operation. Real clinical reasoning ends in a judgment under uncertainty, not in revelation of the canonical answer. Cases that converge teach pattern-matching to the canonical answer; cases that remain ambiguous teach judgment.
- **The pragmatic middle (Thistlethwaite, Newble):** Cases should converge on a *defended reasoning process*, not on a single verdict. The student's product is the reasoning chain. Two students arriving at different verdicts via well-defended reasoning both "pass." The convergence is on the *quality of judgment*, not on the verdict.

For Medhavy: the pragmatic-middle position is the most defensible. Convergence on reasoning quality is gradeable, ambiguity-preserving, and consistent with how clinical reasoning is actually evaluated in OSCEs and oral exams.

### 2.5 Progressive disclosure

Schmidt (1983), "Problem-Based Learning: Rationale and Description," *Medical Education* 17(1): 11–16 — the originating description of PBL's "seven jumps" includes staged information release as a core design element. Information is released in chunks; students reason from incomplete information, generate hypotheses, request more information, refine.

- **Empirical support:** Modest but consistent. Hmelo-Silver (2004), "Problem-Based Learning: What and How Do Students Learn?" *Educational Psychology Review* 16(3): 235–266 — reviews evidence that staged disclosure produces better hypothesis-generation behavior than all-at-once presentation.
- **The trade-off (always name it):** Staged disclosure burns time and increases facilitator effort. A 30-minute case becomes a 90-minute case. Whether it's worth the time depends on how often clinical reasoning actually proceeds that way in the discipline being taught (frequently in internal medicine, less so in radiology).
- **For Medhavy:** Progressive disclosure is straightforward to implement in an AI-facilitated mode (the facilitator can release the next piece of information when the student asks the right question). This is one place where AI facilitation is plausibly *superior* to lecture-hall facilitation, because the timing can be student-specific rather than group-paced.

### 2.6 LCME and AAMC guidance

- **LCME Standard 6.2** (Liaison Committee on Medical Education, *Functions and Structure of a Medical School*) requires that medical school curricula include "active learning and self-directed learning experiences." Case-based formats are explicitly listed as compliant. The standard does not specify CBL vs. PBL vs. TBL.
- **AAMC MedEdPORTAL** is the peer-reviewed repository for medical education materials including cases. Acceptance criteria (described in their published *Reviewer Guidelines*) include: clear learning objectives, evidence base for clinical content (citations to current guidelines), pilot data or implementation evidence, instructor's guide, and faculty review documentation. This is a useful concrete benchmark for what "reviewed" means in medical case work.

## 3. Faculty review and quality control

### 3.1 What review chains look like in existing medical case libraries

- **Aquifer (formerly MedU)** — the case-library cooperative used by ~95% of US medical schools for clinical clerkship cases. Review chain: case authors are clerkship directors at member institutions; cases are reviewed by Aquifer's editorial board (clinical faculty); cases are piloted at multiple institutions; revisions go through a national clinical advisory committee. Currency: cases are reviewed on a rolling 2–3 year cycle. [vendor; structure is publicly documented]
- **NEJM Healer** — published by the *New England Journal of Medicine* group, focused on diagnostic reasoning. Cases are authored and reviewed by NEJM-affiliated clinicians. Review chain is editorially analogous to *NEJM* itself but is not peer-reviewed in the traditional academic-publication sense. [vendor]
- **AAMC MedEdPORTAL** — open-access, peer-reviewed clinical education materials. Submissions undergo formal peer review by clinical educators in the relevant specialty. Acceptance rates are publicly reported (~30–40% historical range). The review criteria are public and serviceable as a rubric for Medhavy. [reference standard]
- **Khan Academy MCAT cases** — Khan's MCAT case library was developed in partnership with the AAMC. Review chain: AAMC subject-matter experts authored and reviewed; Khan edited for platform fit. [vendor]

### 3.2 Review criteria that distinguish excellent from adequate cases

Synthesizing AAMC MedEdPORTAL reviewer guidelines, AMEE Guide 23 (Harden 2001 on assessment), and BEME Guide 23 (Thistlethwaite 2012), the criteria that recurrently distinguish strong from adequate cases:

1. **Clinical accuracy.** Every clinical claim traces to current guidelines (within the past 3–5 years for areas of active change, longer for stable domains). Citations required.
2. **Learning objectives explicit and assessable.** Objectives at Bloom Apply level or above; assessable evidence is named (what the student will produce, what counts as success).
3. **Patient voice present.** The case includes the patient's words, social context, and values, not just lab values. This is the difference between a case and a test question.
4. **Equity and representation review.** AAMC explicitly requires that cases be reviewed for representational harms — racial, gendered, socioeconomic stereotyping in clinical presentations. This is now standard, not optional.
5. **Facilitator's guide.** Excellent cases include a guide with anticipated student responses, common misconceptions, and prompts for the facilitator. Adequate cases include only the case text. This is the single most common gap.
6. **Pilot evidence.** Cases used with real students at least once before national release, with documented revisions based on pilot performance.

### 3.3 Faculty-reviewed vs. unreviewed materials — does the review step matter empirically?

The honest answer: **the review step matters by professional consensus and regulatory requirement, not by direct empirical comparison.** No RCT randomizes students to reviewed vs. unreviewed clinical case content; the experiment would be ethically untenable in clinical education.

What we have is indirect evidence:

- **Medical error in published case content.** Audit studies (e.g., Sebok-Syer et al. 2019, *Academic Medicine*, on case-content accuracy in commonly used materials) routinely find clinical inaccuracies in widely deployed cases — wrong drug doses, outdated guidelines, missing red flags. These audits are themselves part of the review apparatus. The existence of errors in *reviewed* materials sets a lower bound on what unreviewed materials would contain.
- **LLM clinical accuracy ceilings.** Singhal et al. (2023), "Large Language Models Encode Clinical Knowledge," *Nature* 620: 172–180 — Med-PaLM achieves 67% on USMLE-style questions (Med-PaLM 2 achieves ~86%). The non-trivial residual error rate, combined with the *confidence* with which LLMs assert wrong answers, is the empirical case for human review of any LLM-generated clinical content.
- **The Lee et al. (2023) cautions.** Peter Lee, Carey Goldberg, Isaac Kohane (2023), *The AI Revolution in Medicine: GPT-4 and Beyond* — internal Microsoft Research evaluations documenting plausible-sounding but wrong clinical claims from GPT-4. The authors' explicit recommendation: AI-generated clinical content requires human review in proportion to the stakes of the use.

The combined argument: review matters because the alternative is documented to produce errors that propagate as student misconceptions. The argument is not "review has been shown to outperform no-review in RCTs"; it is "no-review has been shown to produce errors and the professional standards require review."

### 3.4 Case currency — how programs handle obsolescence

Clinical guidelines change. Hypertension thresholds changed in 2017 (ACC/AHA), diabetes pharmacotherapy is revised annually by ADA, antibiotic stewardship recommendations are revised by IDSA on rolling cycles. A case that named "first-line treatment" five years ago may be wrong today.

Program practice (as documented in Aquifer's editorial policy and AAMC MedEdPORTAL's review cycle):

- **High-velocity domains** (oncology, infectious disease, cardiology pharmacology) — review annually or biennially.
- **Mid-velocity domains** (internal medicine general management) — review every 2–3 years.
- **Low-velocity domains** (anatomy, basic pathology) — review every 5+ years.

For Medhavy: **case currency is itself a design problem the platform must solve, not delegate.** If cases are pre-written and reviewed by faculty, the review process must include a currency-check cadence per domain. The on-demand generation alternative has a worse failure mode here: an LLM's training cutoff fixes its guideline knowledge in time, and the model has no mechanism to know that a 2023 guideline has been superseded by a 2026 one.

## 4. AI as case facilitator

### 4.1 Socratic facilitation vs. direct explanation — what's the case for Socratic *specifically in case work*?

The generic Socratic-method literature (Paul and Elder 2007, *The Thinker's Guide to Socratic Questioning*) is normative philosophy of education, not empirical. The CBL-specific empirical case for Socratic facilitation is thinner than the rhetoric implies.

What exists:

- **Lake (2001), "Student Performance and Perceptions of a Lecture-Based Course Compared with the Same Course Utilizing Group Discussion,"** *Physical Therapy* 81(3): 896–902 — small-N comparison favoring discussion-based; not specifically Socratic.
- **Tofade, Elsner, and Haines (2013), "Best Practice Strategies for Effective Use of Questions as a Teaching Tool,"** *American Journal of Pharmaceutical Education* 77(7): Article 155 — reviews questioning strategies, supports the use of higher-order questions in case discussion, evidence base is mostly observational.
- **Most positive Socratic-method evidence in education is from K–12 reading comprehension and from law-school doctrinal classes,** not from clinical case discussion. The extrapolation to clinical CBL is defensible on theoretical grounds (Type 2 reasoning under uncertainty benefits from forced verbalization of reasoning) but is not directly evidenced.

### 4.2 The Bastani PNAS 2025 finding — applied to case work [AI-facilitated]

Bastani, Bastani, Sungu, Ge, Kabakcı, and Mariman (2025), "Generative AI Can Harm Learning," *PNAS* (forthcoming/in press at time of cutoff — `[verify]` exact volume/issue/DOI; preprint available via SSRN, "Generative AI Can Harm Learning," SSRN 4895486, 2024).

- **Design:** Field experiment with ~1,000 high-school students learning math. Three conditions: control (no AI), GPT Base (gives answers on request), GPT Tutor (refuses to give answers, scaffolds Socratically).
- **Findings:**
  - During practice with AI access: GPT Base students performed *better* than control; GPT Tutor students performed similar to control.
  - On a *closed-book exam without AI access*: GPT Base students performed *worse* than control by approximately 17%. GPT Tutor students performed similar to control.
- **Mechanism:** Students using GPT Base offloaded problem-solving rather than learned. The performance during practice was the AI's performance, not the student's. The deficit revealed itself when the scaffolding was removed.
- **Application to case work:** The same architecture is plausibly more harmful in case discussion than in math problem-solving, because case reasoning is *exactly* the cognitive operation a learner most wants to offload. The student facing a hard case and an AI that will produce a defensible reasoning chain on request will take the chain. The Bastani finding predicts that the student who used the chain will be worse at clinical reasoning when the chain is unavailable — i.e., in the clinic.
- **For Medhavy's Case Study mode:** This is the strongest empirical argument for the Socratic/scaffolding stance — refuse to give answers, force the student to produce the reasoning, accept the engagement cost. The Khanmigo IDK IDK finding is the same phenomenon viewed from a different angle: students will exit Socratic prompts to retrieve answers if exit paths exist.

### 4.3 AI hallucination risk in clinical material [AI-facilitated]

- **Singhal et al. (2023), Med-PaLM:** Cited above. Median accuracy on USMLE-style questions ~67% (Med-PaLM 1), ~86% (Med-PaLM 2). Even at 86%, one in seven responses is wrong on questions designed to have a single correct answer. Case-based clinical reasoning, where the right answer is more ambiguous, has no equivalent ceiling reported.
- **Lee, Goldberg, and Kohane (2023),** *The AI Revolution in Medicine* — documented multiple examples of GPT-4 producing fluent, plausible, confidently asserted, *wrong* clinical claims. The "I am hallucinating with confidence" failure mode is the defining risk.
- **JAMA editorial caution:** Stead (2023), "Clinical Implications and Challenges of Artificial Intelligence and Deep Learning," *JAMA* — argues that LLM clinical content requires human review and that the burden is on the deploying institution to prove safety, not on critics to prove harm.
- **AAMC position:** AAMC has published guidance (2023–2024) on AI in medical education emphasizing instructor oversight, currency review, and the requirement that AI-generated clinical content not be deployed to students without faculty review. [verify exact AAMC document title/date]

### 4.4 Chatbot vs. human facilitator in case discussion

**The literature is genuinely thin.** What exists is mostly:

- **Usability studies** of conversational agents in medical education (e.g., Holderried et al. 2024, conversational agents for OSCE prep — primarily descriptive, satisfaction-focused).
- **Simulation studies** comparing GPT-4 to standardized patients in clinical interview training — emerging literature, no consensus.
- **No RCT** of AI vs. faculty facilitator on transfer-to-clinic outcomes as of the cutoff date.

For the chapter author: this is a place where Medhavy's honest stance is to name the gap and refuse the easy claim. The strongest argument available is the indirect one: the literature on AI facilitators in other domains (math, writing) is mixed-to-cautionary; the medical-content literature warns against unreviewed AI clinical claims; therefore the design choice (AI facilitator + pre-written case + faculty review) is the conservative position that the existing evidence collectively supports.

### 4.5 The "what's the answer?" problem — Aleven and Koedinger's assistance dilemma

The Medhavy-specific design question: how should an AI case facilitator respond when the student says "I don't know" or "what's the answer?"

The framework is Aleven and Koedinger's **assistance dilemma** — Koedinger and Aleven (2007), "Exploring the Assistance Dilemma in Experiments with Cognitive Tutors," *Educational Psychology Review* 19(3): 239–264.

- **Statement:** "Should educational technology provide more or less assistance?" Either extreme fails. Too little assistance → frustration, abandonment, no learning. Too much assistance → offloading, dependency, no learning (the Bastani finding in advance, by twenty years).
- **The empirical pattern from cognitive tutors:** Optimal assistance is *graduated*. Start by re-asking the question with a hint. Escalate to a partial scaffold. Escalate to a worked example. Never give the final answer without first establishing that the student can verbalize the reasoning that produces it.
- **Operationalization for Medhavy's Case Study mode:**
  - Level 1: "What evidence in the case points one direction or another?"
  - Level 2: "If you had to name two possibilities, what would they be? What evidence would distinguish them?"
  - Level 3: "Here is one reasoning chain a clinician might use. What would you push back on?"
  - Level 4: "Here is the consensus reading. Now reconstruct the reasoning that produced it."
  - Never: "The answer is X."

This graduated structure is consistent with cognitive-flexibility theory, with the Bastani finding, with the Khanmigo failure mode (where exit paths to "just give me the answer" defeated the scaffolding), and with the medical-education tradition of attending-led case discussion where the attending makes the student articulate reasoning before naming the diagnosis.

### 4.6 The reflexive caveat

Every Socratic/scaffolding argument in this section is theoretically motivated, with empirical support mostly from adjacent domains (math, writing). Direct evidence that AI Socratic facilitation in clinical case work produces better clinical reasoning than AI direct-explanation in clinical case work *does not yet exist*. The Bastani extrapolation is the closest, and it is an extrapolation. The chapter should say so.

## 5. Medhavy mode classification

### 5.1 Reading Medhavy's design documents

From `book.md`, `outline.md`, `vision.md`, and `architecture.md`: the planning scaffolds are largely empty (NEEDS HUMAN INPUT throughout). The substantive design is in the chapter drafts, particularly `chapters/01-what-is-medhavy.md` and `chapters/04-what-makes-this-different.md`.

The Case Study mode as described there:

- "**Professor-approved clinical scenarios** presenting genuine ambiguity. Not 'here is the right answer explained through a story' but 'here is a situation with legitimate competing values and incomplete information — what's your reasoning?' The professor approves the cases. The ambiguity is real." (Ch. 1)
- "Case Study activates **causal reasoning and judgment under uncertainty** — appropriate when the representation exists and needs to be applied." (Ch. 4)
- Used as **one of four modes**, selected by the contextual bandit *after* representation has been built through Ask AI / Quiz Me. Not a complete pedagogy, not the curriculum.
- Cases are **pre-authored and faculty-approved**, not generated on demand.
- Sessions are **logged at GLP-relevant granularity** — what type of question, how the student performed, whether they switched modes.

### 5.2 Classification

**Medhavy's Case Study mode most closely inherits from the CBL tradition, not PBL or TBL.** The diagnostic features:

- **Preparation expected** — students arrive at Case Study mode having built representation through other modes (Ask AI, Quiz Me). This is the CBL preparation expectation, not PBL's cold-encounter approach.
- **Faculty-approved cases with defined learning objectives** — CBL's curated-content stance, not PBL's open learning-issues stance.
- **Shorter session cycle** — Medhavy sessions are individual, not multi-day group discussions. This is closer to CBL session length than PBL's week-long arcs.
- **Single-student usage, not group** — neither PBL nor traditional CBL, both of which are group designs. Medhavy's design is solo case work, which is *closer* to CBL than PBL but is structurally novel (most CBL evidence is group-facilitated).
- **One-of-many modes** — neither PBL (which is *the* curriculum) nor TBL (which is *the* class period structure). Medhavy uses cases as one cognitive operation among four. This is unlike all three traditions, and is a Medhavy-specific design choice.

**The cleanest classification: Medhavy's Case Study mode is solo-asynchronous AI-facilitated CBL, with the case library curated under faculty review in the AAMC MedEdPORTAL tradition.** The evidence base that applies most directly is the CBL literature (Thistlethwaite 2012, McLean 2016) — which supports satisfaction and modest skill gains, does not support knowledge-superiority claims, and assumes faculty curation.

### 5.3 Divergences from the canonical CBL evidence base

Medhavy's design diverges from the canonical CBL evidence base on three dimensions worth flagging:

1. **Solo rather than group facilitation.** The CBL evidence base is built on group discussion under faculty facilitation. Medhavy's solo design loses the peer-discussion mechanism that CBL theory credits for some of its effect. Whether the loss is compensated by AI facilitation is an open empirical question; Medhavy is the experiment, not the evidence.
2. **AI rather than human facilitator.** The CBL evidence base assumes a content-expert human facilitator. Medhavy substitutes AI. The Bastani finding suggests that *if* the AI facilitator holds the Socratic line (refuses to give answers, scaffolds graduatedly), the design is at least not catastrophic. The Khanmigo finding suggests that holding the line is harder than designers expect.
3. **Mode used after representation rather than as primary learning vehicle.** CBL studies typically use cases as the primary mechanism for *building* clinical reasoning through repeated cycles. Medhavy uses Case Study *after* representation has been built through other modes. This is closer to a clerkship-style "apply what you know" use of cases than to the curricular-CBL use. The evidence base for this *application use* is sparser than the evidence base for CBL as a learning vehicle, but the design is also less ambitious — Medhavy is not claiming Case Study is the curriculum, only that it is the mode for application.

### 5.4 The evidence base that most applies to Medhavy specifically

- **Thistlethwaite et al. 2012 (BEME Guide 23)** — most directly applicable, with the caveat that it evaluates group-facilitated CBL.
- **Aleven and Koedinger (assistance dilemma; Cognitive Tutor empirical record)** — applies directly to the solo-AI-facilitated design choice. Medhavy's Case Study mode is closer architecturally to a Cognitive Tutor than to a PBL tutorial.
- **Bastani et al. 2025 (PNAS / SSRN)** — applies directly to the Socratic-vs-direct-explanation design choice. The strongest single piece of evidence for Medhavy's "don't give the answer" stance, and itself extrapolated from math to clinical reasoning.
- **Singhal et al. 2023 (Med-PaLM) and Lee et al. 2023** — apply directly to why cases should be pre-authored and faculty-reviewed rather than on-demand generated.

Combined: the CBL literature licenses the broad design (preparation, faculty-curated cases, ambiguity-preserving), the cognitive-tutor literature licenses the assistance architecture (graduated scaffolding, no answer reveal), and the medical-LLM literature licenses the human-review gate.

### 5.5 Open questions Medhavy's design surfaces that the literature does not yet resolve

1. **Does solo AI facilitation produce the clinical-reasoning gains group human facilitation produces?** No direct evidence. Medhavy 1.5 is the data-generation phase.
2. **Does the contextual-bandit selection of Case Study at the right moment for the right learner produce better outcomes than fixed exposure to cases?** No prior platform has tested this; Medhavy 2.0 is the test.
3. **Does the GLP measurement layer detect genuine clinical-reasoning development in case sessions, or only behavioral engagement with case scenarios?** This is the IDK-IDK problem in its case-mode-specific form, and is the central validation question for the whole Medhavy thesis.

## Notes for the chapter author

- **The single cleanest framing for the Medhavy chapter on Case Study mode:** "We are running CBL — Case-Based Learning, McMaster-derivative but post-PBL — with an AI Socratic facilitator instead of a faculty discussion leader, on faculty-curated cases instead of on-demand generated cases, as one of four modes selected by a conductor rather than as the curriculum. The CBL evidence base supports the broad design with caveats; the cognitive-tutor evidence base supports the assistance architecture; the medical-LLM evidence base supports the human-review gate; the direct comparative evidence for *this specific combination* does not yet exist, which is why Medhavy 1.5 is the data generation layer."
- **What to be careful about saying:** Do not say "case-based learning has been shown to improve clinical reasoning." Say "case-based learning has been shown to improve student satisfaction and self-reported clinical reasoning; effects on assessed clinical reasoning are modest and inconsistent across studies." The honest version is the stronger version because it doesn't invite the counterclaim.
- **The Thistlethwaite paragraph is the one to quarry first.** It is the canonical synthesis, it covers all health professions, and its qualitative-modal conclusions ("students like it, faculty find it sustainable, knowledge gains are not consistent, transfer evidence is thin") are the responsible framing for any chapter claim.
