Abstract
Meta-analytic syntheses of educational research — most prominently Hattie's Visible Learning database — have produced ranked lists of instructional interventions ordered by effect size. These rankings have shaped professional development and school policy worldwide. However, the flat list format encodes a fundamental methodological error: it implies that each variable acts independently on student achievement, when in practice educational variables form dense causal networks where the effect of any single intervention depends on what else is present. We propose a research architecture that converts effect size rankings into a directed causal graph where each edge is a testable hypothesis, not a truth claim. Contested or unclear mechanisms are represented as undirected edges — explicitly marking what is unknown rather than averaging it away. We then describe how Medhavy, a one-on-one AI tutoring system, provides an experimental substrate for testing these edges with properties unavailable in traditional classroom research: individual-level random assignment of intervention policies, no spillover between students, and logged treatment characterization at the interaction level. The chatbot functions not only as a general tutor but as a policy executor — delivering a specified pedagogical intervention consistently across students while varying only the dimension under test. This paper describes the architecture and proposed graph. Empirical results from edge-testing experiments will be reported in subsequent work.

1. Introduction
1.1 The promise and problem of effect size synthesis
In [FILL IN: year of first Visible Learning publication], John Hattie published a synthesis of over [FILL IN: number] meta-analyses covering [FILL IN: number] individual studies and [FILL IN: number] students. By standardizing outcomes using Cohen's d and introducing a hinge point of d = 0.40 as the threshold for educationally significant effects, Hattie offered practitioners a seemingly objective basis for prioritizing instructional investments. The ranked list became one of the most cited documents in education policy globally.
The appeal is understandable. Education produces an enormous volume of research with wildly varying quality, and practitioners lack the statistical training to evaluate individual studies. A single ranked list, updated regularly, appears to solve the curation problem.
The problem is that a ranked list implies independence. It suggests that a school can select the top five interventions, implement them, and receive the sum of their effect sizes. This is not how educational causation works. A student's response to feedback depends on whether the feedback references criteria they understand — which depends on how clearly the teacher communicated learning intentions — which depends on whether the student believes the teacher is competent. These are not additive independent causes. They are a causal chain in which each link is a precondition for the next.
Treating them as independent effects is not merely imprecise. It actively misleads: it suggests that schools implementing feedback programs without first establishing teacher clarity will get d = 0.72 on average, when what they will actually get is a classroom where specific, actionable feedback is impossible because the success criteria that would make feedback meaningful were never established.
1.2 The causal inference alternative
Causal inference provides the formal machinery for addressing this problem. Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) — sometimes called causal graphs — represent variables as nodes and causal relationships as directed edges, with the constraint that no variable can be its own ancestor [FILL IN: cite Pearl 2000, Judea Pearl Causality]. The graph encodes researcher beliefs about the data-generating process and makes those beliefs explicit, checkable, and testable.
The key distinction in causal inference is between observation and intervention. The quantity P(outcome | feedback) — the probability of a good outcome given that we observe a student receiving feedback — is contaminated by everything correlated with who naturally receives feedback in a classroom. The quantity P(outcome | do(feedback)) — the probability of a good outcome given that we intervene to assign feedback — is a causal quantity. The DAG framework, specifically the do-calculus developed by [FILL IN: Pearl citation], provides rules for identifying when and how observational data can be used to estimate interventional quantities, and when it cannot.
Converting Hattie's rankings to a DAG is therefore not merely a different visualization of the same information. It is a different epistemological commitment: from "variable X has effect size d on achievement" to "intervening on X causes a change in Y through mechanism M, conditional on the state of confounders C."
1.3 The experimental substrate problem
DAGs are tools for designing and interpreting studies, not for generating findings by themselves. Testing the edges of a pedagogical DAG requires experiments that can isolate individual causal paths. Traditional classroom research faces severe structural obstacles:
* Interventions are delivered by teachers, who bundle multiple variables (clarity, credibility, feedback, relationship quality) that covary because they reflect a single underlying teaching quality
* Students are clustered in classrooms, creating spillover effects where one student's treatment affects another's outcome
* Random assignment at the individual student level is logistically impossible in most school settings
* Treatment exposure is rarely logged with enough precision to characterize what was actually delivered
These obstacles have produced decades of quasi-experimental classroom research that is honest about its limitations but unable to overcome them. Propensity score matching and inverse probability weighting can partially address confounding from observed covariates, but cannot recover the causal quantity from unobserved confounders [FILL IN: cite relevant causal inference reference].
We propose that one-on-one AI tutoring systems provide a solution to the experimental substrate problem that has not been previously exploited for this purpose.
1.4 Contribution of this paper
This paper makes three contributions:
1. We describe a methodology for converting effect size rankings into a directed causal graph where each edge is a falsifiable hypothesis with a named mechanism and a specified statistical test
2. We describe how Medhavy's architecture provides experimental properties — individual-level random assignment, no spillover, logged treatment characterization — that address the core obstacles of classroom pedagogical research
3. We present a proposed causal graph for a subset of Hattie's highest-effect variables, with explicit notation for contested edges
The empirical program of testing these edges is described as future work. This paper is the architecture; the experiments come next.

3. Methods: Graph Construction Methodology
3.1 Node selection criteria
We depart from the obvious approach of selecting Hattie's top 20-30 variables by effect size. Effect size is a measure of statistical association in study samples, not of causal position in a system. The highest-effect variables in the Visible Learning database include outcomes masquerading as inputs: self-reported grades (d = 1.33) is essentially a measure of how well students can predict their own performance from prior performance — a correlate of everything upstream, not a manipulable cause. Including it as an intervention target would produce a graph with a collider at the outcome node rather than a causal chain.
We instead select nodes using three criteria:
Manipulability. A node must represent something that a teacher or instructional system can directly vary. Teacher clarity and feedback quality are manipulable. Student socioeconomic status and prior knowledge are not — they belong in the graph as exogenous background variables that must be conditioned on, not as intervention targets.
Causal role. Nodes must be classified before graph construction as: (a) exogenous inputs — variables that influence outcomes but are not caused by other variables in the system, (b) manipulable causes — the actual intervention nodes, (c) intermediate states — cognitive or motivational states that transmit causal effects between interventions and outcomes, or (d) proximal outcomes — measurable states close to the causal mechanism, distinct from the distal achievement outcome. Conflating these categories is the primary source of graph misspecification.
Temporal ordering. Edges can only run forward in time. Variables are assigned to the phase of instruction in which they operate: instructional design decisions before the lesson, delivery variables during instruction, cognitive states during and after, and measured outcomes at assessment.
3.2 Mechanism specification
Each directed edge in the graph requires a mechanism specification — a statement of how the cause produces the effect, naming the cognitive or behavioral process that transmits the influence. "Feedback improves achievement" is an association claim. "Timely corrective feedback reduces misconception persistence by replacing the erroneous retrieval cue before it consolidates in long-term memory" is a mechanism claim. Only the latter generates testable predictions about what moderates the effect, what the optimal timing should be, and what a null result would mean.
We classify mechanisms into four families:
* Cognitive load mechanisms: effects that operate through working memory capacity and management (e.g., managing intrinsic, extraneous, and germane load)
* Memory consolidation mechanisms: effects that operate through encoding, retrieval, and interference in long-term memory (e.g., spacing effects, testing effects, interference reduction)
* Metacognitive mechanisms: effects that operate through self-monitoring, calibration, and goal-setting (e.g., self-assessment accuracy, goal difficulty calibration)
* Motivational mechanisms: effects that operate through expectancy-value, attribution, and self-efficacy (e.g., competence beliefs, growth mindset)
This taxonomy matters because mechanisms in different families predict different experimental signatures. Two interventions that both improve achievement through different mechanisms will have different dose-response shapes, different moderators, and different interaction patterns. Conflating them in a single "feedback works" finding is the analytical error that this architecture is designed to prevent.
3.3 Edge directionality notation
We use three edge types:
* Directed edge (→): Mechanism hypothesized, literature reasonably convergent, direction identifiable
* Contested edge (⇢): Two or more plausible mechanisms predict the effect but make different predictions about moderators, dose-response shape, or boundary conditions. These are the primary experimental targets.
* Reversed candidate (⟵?): The variable may be an outcome rather than a cause — annotated for removal from the intervention set pending analysis
Contested edges are not a limitation of the graph — they are its primary scientific product. Each contested edge is an experiment specification: "we do not know whether mechanism A or mechanism B is operative here; designing a study that can distinguish them requires varying [X] while holding [Y] constant."

4. The Medhavy Experimental Architecture
4.1 The chatbot as policy executor
The framing that makes Medhavy useful for this research program is the distinction between a tutor and a policy executor. A tutor exercises judgment: it reads the student, selects an approach, adjusts tone, chooses examples. This judgment is valuable for student outcomes but catastrophic for measurement — it means each student receives a different bundle of interventions, and the treatment cannot be defined precisely enough to interpret results.
A policy executor receives a specification: "When the student produces an incorrect answer to a conceptual question, deliver corrective feedback that (a) names the specific criterion violated, (b) provides one counterexample that directly addresses the error, and (c) asks the student to re-attempt without providing the answer." It executes this specification consistently across students while varying only the dimension under experimental test. The pedagogical intelligence lives in the causal graph and the experimental design, not in the chatbot's judgment.
This reframing resolves a common concern about using LLMs in educational research: that their stochasticity makes treatment definition impossible. Stochasticity in how a specified policy is expressed is analogous to variance in how a human pharmacist explains a medication — it is within-treatment noise, not treatment ambiguity, as long as the policy is defined at the level of the causally active ingredient. The logs provide post-hoc treatment characterization to verify that each student's actual interaction fell within the specified policy space.
4.2 Random assignment and the severing of backdoor paths
In a standard observational classroom study, the treatment (feedback quality) is correlated with confounders (teacher quality, student attentiveness, prior knowledge) because these variables share common causes. This opens backdoor paths from confounders through treatment to outcome that inflate or deflate the estimated effect of the treatment. Statistical adjustment — regression, propensity score matching, inverse probability weighting — can close these paths but only for observed confounders. Unobserved confounders remain a permanent threat to causal interpretation.
Medhavy can assign interventions randomly. A student in the corrective-specificity-high condition receives high-specificity feedback not because they are a high-attentiveness student whose teacher happens to give good feedback, but because the experimental design assigned them to that condition. The correlation between treatment and confounders is broken by design. Backdoor paths are severed before data collection, not adjusted away afterward. The estimated effect is the interventional quantity P(outcome | do(feedback specificity = high)) directly, without identifying assumptions about unobserved confounders.
This is a stronger causal claim than any observational study of classroom feedback can make, regardless of how sophisticated its adjustment strategy.
4.3 SUTVA satisfaction
The Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption (SUTVA) requires two conditions: (a) no interference between units — the treatment assigned to student A does not affect the outcome of student B — and (b) no multiple versions of treatment — "receives high-specificity feedback" means the same thing for all students in that condition [FILL IN: cite Rubin 1980 or relevant SUTVA reference].
Interference (condition a): One-on-one architecture eliminates the peer spillover that makes SUTVA problematic in classroom RCTs. Student A has no knowledge of what Student B is experiencing. There is no mechanism through which A's treatment assignment affects B's outcome.
Multiple versions (condition b): This condition is not fully satisfied but is made tractable by the logging architecture. Two students in the same treatment condition receive different natural-language realizations of the same policy — different word choices, different examples, different lengths. The question is whether these differences represent multiple versions of the treatment (a SUTVA violation) or within-treatment noise (acceptable variance).
We address this through treatment characterization from logs. Each interaction is coded along the dimensions that the causal model identifies as the active ingredient: specificity (did the feedback reference the specific criterion violated?), timing (latency between error and feedback delivery), elaboration type (corrective vs. confirmatory), and worked example inclusion. This characterization allows dose-response analysis in which the causal model is tested not just at the treatment-assignment level but at the level of what was actually delivered — partially recovering the stable treatment values assumption from log data.
4.4 Path isolation by experimental control
The bundling problem in classroom research — that teachers deliver multiple variables simultaneously because they reflect a single underlying teaching quality — is resolved by experimental control in Medhavy. The system can hold feedback timing constant while varying specificity; hold specificity constant while varying elaboration ratio; hold both constant while varying whether worked examples precede or follow correction.
This path isolation is what converts the proposed causal graph from a theoretical structure into an experimental program. Each edge in the graph corresponds to an experiment in which the source variable is manipulated, other variables on paths through the graph are held constant, and the outcome is measured. The result either confirms the edge (supports the mechanism), produces a null that is interpretable given the conditioning set (the mechanism may be absent), or produces a pattern that distinguishes between competing mechanisms (resolves a contested edge).

5. Anticipated Results and Interpretation Framework
5.1 Possible outcomes for each edge test
A well-designed edge test has three interpretable outcomes:
Confirmed edge: The intervention causes the predicted change in the target node with the predicted dose-response shape and the predicted moderator pattern. This does not prove the mechanism — it is consistent with it. Mechanism confirmation requires ruling out alternative causal stories, which typically requires additional experiments.
Null result: No detectable effect at the pre-specified power level. This is interpretable only if the treatment characterization from logs confirms that the specified policy was actually delivered. A null with confirmed delivery implicates the mechanism. A null with variable delivery implicates the operationalization — the right mechanism may have been targeted with the wrong execution.
Pattern inconsistent with mechanism: The effect exists but the moderator pattern, dose-response shape, or boundary conditions are inconsistent with the hypothesized mechanism. This is the most scientifically productive outcome: it rules out the hypothesized mechanism while establishing that the edge exists, generating a new contested-edge annotation and a more refined experimental question.
5.2 How results update the graph
The graph is a living document. Each experiment produces one of three graph updates:
* Directed edge confirmed: The contested annotation is removed; the edge is directed with a mechanism tag
* Directed edge disconfirmed: The edge is marked with the falsifying result; the alternate mechanism becomes the primary hypothesis
* Contested edge refined: The undirected edge is replaced by a more specifically defined contested edge between two now-better-specified competing mechanisms
Over time, the graph converges toward a more fully directed structure — not because we assumed the mechanisms in advance, but because we tested them sequentially. The undirected portions of the graph at any point represent the current experimental frontier: what Medhavy is currently running.

7. Discussion
7.1 The graph as scientific program
The contribution of this paper is not a set of findings. It is a research architecture — a formalized way of asking questions about educational causation that can be tested sequentially, updated as results accumulate, and shared across research groups. The causal graph is not a theory of education; it is a structured set of hypotheses about education, annotated with their epistemic status and the experiments required to resolve them.
This is the appropriate relationship between Hattie's corpus and educational science. The meta-analytic synthesis established that certain variables are associated with achievement at meaningful magnitudes. It did not establish why, through what mechanisms, conditional on what other variables, or in which sequence. The DAG architecture makes those questions precise. The Medhavy experimental layer makes them answerable.

8. Conclusion
We have described an architecture for converting effect size rankings into a testable causal program. The core moves are three: specifying mechanisms at the edge level rather than the node level, using contested edges to represent genuine scientific uncertainty rather than averaging it away, and deploying Medhavy's one-on-one AI tutoring architecture to test edges with experimental properties unavailable in classroom research.
The graph we have proposed is not a theory. It is a set of bets — specific, falsifiable, sequentially testable bets about how teaching causes learning. Some will be confirmed, some will be disconfirmed, and the most interesting ones will produce patterns that require the graph to be restructured. That is not a failure mode. That is what scientific progress looks like.
The empirical program — the experiments themselves — will be reported in subsequent papers as results become available. This paper provides the architecture those experiments will test.
